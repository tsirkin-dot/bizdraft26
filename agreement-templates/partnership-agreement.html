What a partnership agreement is for

Go into business together. With the rules written down.

A partnership agreement is the founding document of a general or limited partnership. It governs who owns what, how decisions are made, what happens when a partner wants out, and how profits and losses are allocated. Without one, state default rules govern — and they rarely reflect what partners actually intended.

Who uses it

Two or more individuals starting a business together as a general or limited partnership. Professional service firms. Investment partnerships. Co-founders who have not yet formed an LLC or corporation.

When to use it

Before any money is invested, any work is started, or any client is signed. The hardest conversations — equity splits, decision-making authority, what happens if someone wants to leave — are much easier to have before there is money on the table.

When not to use it

Not the right document for businesses formed as LLCs — use an LLC operating agreement. Not suitable for co-founder relationships in a corporation — use a shareholders' agreement or co-founder agreement with equity vesting instead.

Common mistakes

Where partnerships fall apart.

The most common reasons business partnerships end in disputes instead of mutual success.

Mistake

No buyout formula

When a partner wants to leave, "we'll figure it out then" never works. Agree on the valuation method before anyone wants out — and it needs to be binding.

Mistake

50/50 with no deadlock mechanism

Equal partnerships need a deadlock-breaker — a third-party mediator, a tie-breaking vote procedure, or a buy-sell mechanism. Without one, the business can become ungovernable.

Mistake

No non-compete on exit

A partner who leaves can immediately compete using the business's client relationships and methods unless the agreement restricts it. Non-compete and non-solicitation on exit protect the continuing partners.

Mistake

Unequal contributions with equal profit splits

Partners who contribute unequally but split 50/50 often resent each other within a year. Document contributions and tie profit allocation to actual contribution or ongoing effort.

When to ask a lawyer

Where AI ends, counsel begins.

BizDraft is a drafting tool. It is not a substitute for a lawyer who knows your specific situation. Reach for one when:

Significant assets or liabilities

When partners are contributing significant assets, assuming debt, or when the business will hold real property, attorney review protects each partner's interests independently.

Complex equity or compensation structures

Sweat equity, vesting schedules, preferred return provisions, and complex profit-interest arrangements require careful tax and legal structuring beyond what a template provides.

If an LLC might be better

General partnerships expose partners to unlimited personal liability. An attorney can advise whether an LLC (with an operating agreement) is a better structure for your specific situation.

FAQ

Partnership agreement questions.

What happens if there is no partnership agreement?

State law fills in the gaps — and it rarely reflects what the partners intended. Most states' default partnership rules provide for equal profit sharing regardless of contribution, each partner having equal management rights, and the ability of any partner to dissolve the partnership at any time. A written agreement overrides all of this.

Can one partner bind the partnership to a contract without the others?

In a general partnership, yes — each partner is generally an agent of the partnership and can bind it to contracts in the ordinary course of business. The partnership agreement can limit this by requiring approval for contracts above a specified dollar threshold or certain categories of decisions.

What is the difference between a general and limited partnership?

In a general partnership, all partners have unlimited personal liability and management rights. In a limited partnership, there are general partners (unlimited liability, management authority) and limited partners (liability limited to their investment, no management role). LPs require state registration; GPs can be formed by agreement alone.

How are partnership profits taxed?

Partnerships are pass-through entities — profits and losses flow through to the partners' individual tax returns. The partnership itself does not pay federal income tax but must file an informational Form 1065. Each partner receives a K-1 showing their share of income and loss. Consult a CPA for the tax implications of your specific allocation.
