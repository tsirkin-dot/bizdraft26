What an employment agreement is for

Hire the right way. In writing.

An employment agreement defines the terms of a W-2 employment relationship — compensation, title, benefits, work location, IP assignment, confidentiality, and termination terms. It sets expectations on both sides and provides legal protection when either the employer or employee needs to enforce their rights.

Who uses it

Employers hiring full-time or part-time W-2 employees. Startups making their first hires. Any company that wants a written record of the employment terms beyond the offer letter.

When to use it

Before the first day of work. An employment agreement signed at onboarding covers IP assignment and confidentiality from day one — critical for any employee who will touch proprietary information or create valuable work product.

When not to use it

Not for independent contractors — use an IC agreement. Not appropriate as the primary protection for senior executives or C-suite employees — those require specialized employment counsel due to equity, severance, and change-of-control provisions.

Common mistakes

Where employment agreements create problems.

Common drafting errors that create liability or leave IP unprotected.

Mistake

No IP assignment

Without a signed IP assignment, the law in many states gives employees ownership of inventions created without company resources — even on company time. Protect IP from day one.

Mistake

Mixing at-will with for-cause language

Contracts that say "at-will" but also list termination grounds can be read to limit at-will rights. Be intentional — at-will or for-cause, not both.

Mistake

Non-competes in prohibited states

California, North Dakota, Minnesota, and other states ban or severely limit employee non-competes. Including them creates false expectations and potential liability.

Mistake

No prior inventions exclusion

An IP assignment without a prior inventions schedule lets employers claim ownership of work the employee created before being hired. Always include a prior inventions exclusion list.

When to ask a lawyer

Where AI ends, counsel begins.

BizDraft is a drafting tool. It is not a substitute for a lawyer who knows your specific situation. Reach for one when:

Executive and C-suite hires

Senior executive employment agreements involve equity, change-of-control provisions, severance packages, and board rights that require specialized employment counsel on both sides.

State-specific compliance

California, New York, and other states have robust employee-protection laws that limit what employers can require. Have a local employment attorney review the agreement for the employee's work state.

Dispute or termination situation

If you are amending an existing agreement, terminating for cause, or dealing with an employment dispute, stop drafting and consult an employment attorney before taking any action.

FAQ

Employment agreement questions.

Is an employment agreement required by law?

No — employment can exist without a written agreement. But without one, you have no IP assignment, no confidentiality obligations, and no documented at-will terms. The offer letter alone is not enough to protect the employer's IP or the employee's compensation terms.

Can I require a non-compete in the employment agreement?

Depends on the state. California, North Dakota, Minnesota, and several others ban employee non-competes almost entirely. The FTC attempted a federal ban. For states where they are permitted, enforceability depends on the geographic scope, duration, and the employee's role. BizDraft will flag your state's rules.

What does "at-will employment" mean?

At-will means either party can end the employment relationship at any time, for any lawful reason, or for no reason at all. The employer can fire without cause; the employee can quit without notice. Most US states default to at-will unless the agreement says otherwise.

Who owns code written by an employee at home on their personal computer?

Potentially the employer — if the code relates to the company's business or was developed using company information. Many states have employee invention statutes that limit employer claims on truly personal projects. The agreement should clarify what is and is not company property. A prior inventions schedule protects the employee's pre-existing personal projects.
