What an LLC operating agreement is for

Form your LLC. Define how it runs.

An LLC operating agreement is the governing document for a limited liability company. It defines member ownership, management authority, how capital is contributed and profits are distributed, what approval is needed for major decisions, and how members can sell or transfer their interest. Without one, your state's default LLC rules apply.

Who uses it

Single-member LLC owners who want to document their entity's structure for banks, investors, and contracts. Multi-member LLCs where the ownership and governance rules need to be written down before a dispute arises.

When to use it

When you form the LLC or shortly after. Banks require it to open a business account. Investors want to review it before committing capital. Courts need it to confirm the liability shield is real. Do not wait.

When not to use it

Not appropriate for corporations — use bylaws and a shareholders' agreement. Not a substitute for forming the LLC itself — the operating agreement governs the LLC after formation, not instead of it.

Common mistakes

Where operating agreements fail LLCs.

The most common reasons LLC members end up in court — and how a good operating agreement prevents it.

Mistake

No operating agreement at all

Operating without an agreement means state default rules govern your LLC — equal membership voting regardless of ownership percentage, unanimous consent for major decisions, and automatic dissolution on member departure in some states.

Mistake

No tax distribution clause

LLC profits pass through to members who owe tax on them whether or not cash is distributed. Without a mandatory tax distribution, members can owe tens of thousands in tax with no cash from the LLC to pay it.

Mistake

No transfer restrictions

Without transfer restrictions, a member can sell their interest to a stranger and that person becomes your co-owner. A right of first refusal gives existing members the option to buy before outside parties can enter.

Mistake

Using a generic internet template

Operating agreements must comply with your formation state's LLC act. Generic templates often omit state-specific required provisions or include provisions that are void under your state's law.

When to ask a lawyer

Where AI ends, counsel begins.

BizDraft is a drafting tool. It is not a substitute for a lawyer who knows your specific situation. Reach for one when:

Raising investment capital

When investors are entering the LLC, the operating agreement needs preferred-return provisions, anti-dilution protections, information rights, and other investor terms that require legal structuring.

Complex equity structures

Profits interests, different classes of membership, vesting schedules for sweat equity, and carried interest structures require a tax attorney and securities counsel to structure correctly.

Member dispute or exit

If a member wants out and the parties disagree on valuation or the buyout process, get legal counsel before taking any action. Member disputes are easier to resolve with counsel than after positions have hardened.

FAQ

LLC operating agreement questions.

Is an operating agreement required by law?

Not by federal law, but by most states' LLC acts it is strongly recommended and in some states (New York, California) required or assumed to exist. Without one, banks won't open accounts, sophisticated counterparties won't sign contracts, and courts may not fully honor the liability shield.

Does a single-member LLC need an operating agreement?

Yes. A single-member operating agreement establishes that the LLC is a separate legal entity from you personally, reinforces the liability protection, is required by banks to open a business account, and defines what happens to the LLC if you die or become incapacitated.

What is the difference between member-managed and manager-managed?

In a member-managed LLC, all members participate in day-to-day management. In a manager-managed LLC, one or more designated managers (who may or may not be members) run the business while passive members invest. Manager-managed is common when there are passive investors who don't want operational authority or liability.

How are LLC members taxed?

By default, LLCs are pass-through entities — single-member LLCs are disregarded entities (reported on Schedule C), multi-member LLCs are taxed as partnerships (Form 1065, K-1s). Members pay tax on their share of LLC income whether or not it is distributed. An LLC can elect to be taxed as an S-Corp or C-Corp — consult a CPA before doing so.
