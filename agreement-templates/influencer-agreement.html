What an influencer agreement is for

Brand deals. Documented properly.

An influencer agreement governs the commercial relationship between a brand and a content creator. It defines exactly what content will be produced, when, the required FTC disclosure language, how long the brand can reuse the content, whether the influencer can work with competitors, and how payment is structured.

Who uses it

Brands and agencies commissioning influencer content. Influencers, content creators, and YouTubers signing brand deals. Both sides benefit from a signed agreement before content creation begins.

When to use it

Before any brief is sent. Before product is shipped. A signed agreement protects the influencer from brands who change direction mid-campaign and protects brands from influencers who post non-compliant content.

When not to use it

Not appropriate for unpaid gifting without posting obligations — those require a gift agreement. This template is for paid content partnerships with defined deliverables.

Common mistakes

Where influencer deals go wrong.

The most common disputes in influencer partnerships — from the brand's side and the creator's side.

Mistake

No FTC disclosure requirement

The FTC requires clear and conspicuous disclosure of paid partnerships. Failure to disclose can result in FTC enforcement against both the brand and the influencer. The contract must require proper disclosure.

Mistake

Unlimited usage rights

An influencer agreeing to "unlimited" usage has given away the right to ever license that content again. Limit usage rights to specific platforms, uses, and durations.

Mistake

No kill-fee for brand cancellations

Brands sometimes cancel campaigns after the influencer has blocked their calendar, turned down other deals, and started production. A kill-fee compensates for this.

Mistake

Vague exclusivity scope

"Cannot work with competitors" means nothing without naming the category. Is it the entire wellness industry or just protein powder brands? Be specific or exclusivity is unenforceable.

When to ask a lawyer

Where AI ends, counsel begins.

BizDraft is a drafting tool. It is not a substitute for a lawyer who knows your specific situation. Reach for one when:

High-value celebrity campaigns

Deals over $50,000 or involving celebrities and macro-influencers with management teams typically require attorney involvement on both sides.

Long-term ambassador deals

Multi-year brand ambassador agreements with ongoing exclusivity, equity, or revenue-share components are complex enough to warrant attorney drafting.

Regulated product categories

Alcohol, tobacco, gambling, pharmaceuticals, and financial products have specific FTC and FDA advertising rules that affect what influencers can and cannot say. Legal review before the campaign is essential.

FAQ

Influencer agreement questions.

Is #ad disclosure legally required?

Yes. The FTC requires clear and conspicuous disclosure of any material connection — including payment, free product, or equity — between a brand and a content creator. "Clear and conspicuous" means prominent placement, not buried in hashtags. The agreement must require compliant disclosure.

Who owns the content after posting?

The influencer retains copyright ownership of the content. The brand gets a license to use it for specified purposes and duration. The agreement defines the scope of that license — if usage rights are not explicitly granted, the brand cannot repurpose the content for paid ads or other channels.

What is a morality clause and should I include one?

A morality clause allows the brand to terminate the agreement if the influencer engages in conduct that could harm the brand's reputation. Influencers should negotiate a cure period (an opportunity to address the situation) before termination is effective. Both sides have legitimate interests here.

Can the influencer keep the product if the deal falls through?

Only if the agreement says so. If the brand ships product before content is created and then the deal falls through, the agreement should address whether the product is returned or deducted from any kill-fee owed.
