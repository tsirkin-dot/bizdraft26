What a social media management agreement is for

Manage their channels. With clear rules.

A social media management agreement defines the working relationship when a manager or agency takes over a client's social accounts. It covers what gets posted, how often, who approves it, who owns the audience, and what happens to the accounts when the relationship ends.

Who uses it

Social media managers, digital marketing agencies, content creators managing brand accounts, and virtual assistants providing social media services to business clients.

When to use it

Before you get admin access to any client account. Sharing login credentials or account access without a signed agreement exposes both parties to risk — for the client's brand and for your liability.

When not to use it

Not suitable for one-off campaign creation without ongoing account management — use a freelance or marketing services agreement instead. This template is for ongoing account stewardship.

Common mistakes

Where social media management contracts fail.

The most common disputes between social media managers and their clients.

Mistake

Sharing master passwords

Sharing the client's account password rather than using platform admin access creates security and liability risks. Always use native access delegation (Meta Business Manager, LinkedIn Page Admin, etc.).

Mistake

No content approval deadline

When a client takes a week to approve a post that was due yesterday, the manager misses the posting schedule. A deemed-approved clause prevents the calendar from falling apart.

Mistake

Guaranteeing follower growth

Platform algorithms, not managers, determine reach. Contracts that guarantee follower counts or engagement rates create liability for things no professional can control.

Mistake

No off-boarding plan

When the relationship ends, the manager must be removed from all accounts immediately. Define the access revocation process and the timeline for delivering a content archive.

When to ask a lawyer

Where AI ends, counsel begins.

BizDraft is a drafting tool. It is not a substitute for a lawyer who knows your specific situation. Reach for one when:

Paid social campaigns included

When you manage paid ad spend on social platforms in addition to organic content, you need ad-spend authorization clauses similar to a marketing services agreement.

Crisis management authority

If you need authority to respond to a PR crisis in real time without client approval, the scope of that authority and the liability for crisis responses should be attorney-reviewed.

Data access and privacy

Access to follower data, DMs, and analytics may have privacy implications. Enterprise clients with CCPA, GDPR, or HIPAA obligations need specific data handling clauses.

FAQ

Social media management agreement questions.

Who owns the social media followers?

The client — always. The social accounts, the audience, and all historical content belong to the client. The manager is a steward managing the accounts on the client's behalf. This should be explicit in the agreement so there is no confusion when the relationship ends.

What if I post something the client doesn't like?

If the content was approved by the client before posting, the client's recourse is limited to requesting it be taken down. If it was posted without approval and was outside the agreed guidelines, the manager may have liability. This is why the approval workflow must be documented and followed.

Can a social media manager be held responsible for a brand crisis?

Potentially, if approved content was factually false or violated platform policies the manager should have known about. The limitation of liability clause in your agreement caps your exposure. Don't post anything you haven't verified is accurate.

What happens to scheduled posts when the contract ends?

The agreement should address this. On termination, the manager should deliver all drafted and scheduled content, remove it from any third-party scheduling tools, and transfer or delete access. The client keeps their accounts and all historical content.
