What a non-compete agreement is for

Protect your competitive advantage. With the right scope.

A non-compete agreement restricts a departing employee, contractor, or business seller from competing against the party they worked for or sold to. Enforceability varies dramatically by state — BizDraft generates non-competes calibrated to your state's standards so they have a real chance of holding up.

Who uses it

Employers seeking to protect client relationships and trade secrets when key employees leave. Business buyers requiring the seller to not re-enter the market. Companies protecting their competitive position after partnerships end.

When to use it

At employment onboarding for employees with access to trade secrets or key client relationships. As part of a business sale. As a standalone agreement for senior employees or key contractors whose departure could harm the business.

When not to use it

Do not use for California, North Dakota, Minnesota, or Oklahoma employees — non-competes are banned or nearly unenforceable there. Do not use for low-wage workers — many states prohibit non-competes for hourly or below-threshold wage employees.

Non-compete clause

Specific restriction on working for, owning, or consulting for defined competitors within the geographic area for the restriction period.

Non-solicitation clause

Restriction on soliciting the company's clients, prospects, and employees for a defined period — often survives in states where geographic non-competes do not.

Consideration acknowledgment

Explicit acknowledgment by the restricted party of the consideration received — required in many states for a non-compete signed after employment begins.

Protectable interest definition

Statement of the legitimate business interest being protected — trade secrets, client relationships, specialized training — required for enforceability in most states.

Blue-pencil savings clause

If a court finds the scope too broad, it may reduce it rather than void the entire agreement — the blue-pencil clause invites courts to modify rather than strike.

Injunctive relief clause

Acknowledgment that breach would cause irreparable harm, entitling the company to seek injunctive relief without posting bond — critical for enforcement.

Common mistakes

Why non-competes get thrown out.

The most common reasons non-compete agreements fail to hold up when enforced.

Mistake

Scope is too broad

A nationwide non-compete for a sales rep who only ever worked one region, or a restriction covering all industries rather than the company's specific business, will fail in most courts.

Mistake

No legitimate business interest

Courts require a protectable interest — trade secrets, confidential client lists, or specialized training investments. A non-compete that protects nothing legitimate is unenforceable.

Mistake

Signed mid-employment without new consideration

In many states, a non-compete signed after the employee has already started working requires new consideration — a raise, a promotion, a bonus. Just continued employment is not enough.

Mistake

Wrong state's law

Applying Texas law to a California employee does not make California's ban disappear. Courts apply the law of the state where the employee works, regardless of what the contract says.

When to ask a lawyer

Where AI ends, counsel begins.

BizDraft is a drafting tool. It is not a substitute for a lawyer who knows your specific situation. Reach for one when:

Active enforcement situation

If a former employee has violated or is about to violate a non-compete, stop drafting and call an employment attorney immediately. Injunctive relief has strict timing requirements.

Multi-state workforce

When employees work across multiple states, enforceability becomes a patchwork. A uniform non-compete may be unenforceable for some employees. Get state-specific advice.

Business sale non-competes

Seller non-competes in M&A transactions have different legal standards than employment non-competes — they are generally enforceable with broader scope. Attorney drafting is appropriate.

FAQ

Non-compete agreement questions.

Are non-compete agreements enforceable?

Depends on the state. California, North Dakota, Minnesota, and Oklahoma ban employee non-competes nearly entirely. In states where they are permitted, enforceability requires: reasonable scope and duration, a legitimate business interest to protect, and adequate consideration. Courts routinely narrow or void overly broad non-competes.

How long can a non-compete last?

For employees, 6–24 months is the range courts accept in most states. Restrictions longer than 2 years are frequently reduced or voided. Business-sale non-competes can run 3–5 years with broader geographic scope. BizDraft will suggest a duration calibrated to your state and the employee's role.

What is the difference between a non-compete and a non-solicitation agreement?

A non-compete restricts working for or starting a competing business. A non-solicitation restricts approaching the company's clients or employees. Non-solicitation agreements are generally more enforceable than geographic non-competes and are permitted in many states that ban non-competes.

Does the employee have to sign a non-compete to start work?

You can make it a condition of employment. The clearest approach is to include it in the employment offer and have it signed before the first day — then the offer itself is the consideration. Requiring signature after employment starts requires additional consideration in many states.
