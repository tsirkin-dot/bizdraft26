What a sales agreement is for

Sell with confidence. Every term in writing.

A sales agreement documents the terms of a transaction — what is being sold, at what price, how and when it will be delivered, what warranty applies, and how payment is structured. It turns a purchase order or handshake into a legally binding transaction record.

Who uses it

Businesses selling products, equipment, or services in transactions where price, delivery, or warranty terms need to be documented. B2B sales with custom terms, installment payment plans, or specific delivery requirements.

When to use it

For any sale above a few hundred dollars where there is a reasonable chance of a delivery dispute, warranty claim, or payment disagreement. The agreement is your defense against "that's not what I ordered."

When not to use it

Not needed for simple retail point-of-sale transactions with standard return policies. Use a service agreement rather than a sales agreement when the primary deliverable is services, not goods.

Product description and specifications

Detailed description of what is being sold, quantity, specifications, and condition. The reference point for any dispute about whether what was delivered matches what was ordered.

Price and payment schedule

Total price, applicable taxes, deposit requirement, payment milestones, due dates, and late payment consequences.

Delivery terms

Delivery date or window, delivery method, delivery address, risk-of-loss transfer point (FOB origin or destination), and what happens on delivery delay.

Title and risk of loss

When title transfers from seller to buyer and when the buyer bears the risk of damage or loss during transit.

Warranty clause

Express warranty duration and scope — or disclaimer of implied warranties of merchantability and fitness for a particular purpose where the seller wants to limit warranty exposure.

Returns and remedies

Defect inspection period, return authorization process, remedy options (repair, replace, refund), and limitation on consequential damages.

Common mistakes

Where sales agreements create disputes.

The most common reasons sales transactions turn into collection problems or warranty disputes.

Mistake

Risk of loss not specified

Without a clear risk-of-loss clause, both sides may argue the other bore the risk when goods were damaged in transit. Specify FOB origin or FOB destination.

Mistake

Warranty scope undefined

A warranty without a defined duration, scope, and exclusions invites endless claims. Be specific about what you warrant, for how long, and what you do not warrant.

Mistake

No inspection period

Without a defined inspection and rejection period, the buyer can claim defects months after delivery. Require inspection within a defined window and provide that silence constitutes acceptance.

Mistake

No late payment provision

Installment sales without a late-payment clause and a clear default-and-acceleration provision leave the seller without leverage when payments stop.

When to ask a lawyer

Where AI ends, counsel begins.

BizDraft is a drafting tool. It is not a substitute for a lawyer who knows your specific situation. Reach for one when:

High-value transactions

Sales over $250,000, particularly for equipment or specialized goods, should have attorney review of the warranty, liability, and risk-of-loss provisions.

Real property included

If the sale includes real estate or fixtures permanently attached to real property, a real estate attorney must be involved — UCC Article 2 does not govern real property sales.

Product liability risk

Selling goods that could injure users if they malfunction — tools, equipment, consumables — requires product liability consideration in the warranty and indemnification clauses.

FAQ

Sales agreement questions.

What is the difference between a sales agreement and a purchase order?

A purchase order is the buyer's offer to buy — it specifies quantity, price, and delivery. A sales agreement is a negotiated bilateral contract setting terms that bind both sides. For recurring relationships, a sales agreement (with standard terms) backed by individual POs is the most efficient structure.

What does FOB mean?

Free on Board — it defines where risk of loss transfers. FOB origin (seller's location) means risk passes to the buyer when the seller hands the goods to the carrier. FOB destination means the seller bears risk until goods arrive at the buyer's location. For high-value goods, FOB destination protects the buyer.

Can I disclaim implied warranties?

Yes, in most B2B transactions. UCC Article 2 allows disclaimer of the implied warranty of merchantability (goods are fit for their ordinary purpose) and fitness for a particular purpose, as long as the disclaimer is conspicuous (usually in caps or bold). Consumer sales have more restrictions.

What happens if the buyer doesn't pay on an installment plan?

The agreement should include an acceleration clause — if any installment is missed, the entire unpaid balance becomes immediately due. The seller may also have repossession rights if a security interest was retained. Make sure these remedies are in the agreement before the first installment is due.
