What a marketing services agreement is for

Run campaigns. Own the results. Get paid.

A marketing services agreement governs the relationship between a marketing agency or consultant and their client. It defines campaign deliverables, KPIs, content ownership, ad-spend authority, and the limits of performance guarantees — the clauses that prevent disputes when campaigns underperform.

Who uses it

Marketing agencies, freelance marketers, PPC managers, SEO firms, content agencies, email marketing specialists, and any provider running campaigns on behalf of a client.

When to use it

Before the first campaign brief. Before any ad budget is handed over. Before you write the first piece of content. A signed agreement is how you establish who controls what — and who's responsible for results.

When not to use it

Not suitable when the engagement is purely advisory (use a consulting agreement) or when it is a single social media post without ongoing campaign management (use a freelance agreement).

Common mistakes

Where marketing contracts create disputes.

The most common causes of disagreement between agencies and clients when campaigns run.

Mistake

Guaranteeing results

No agency can guarantee ad performance, rankings, or revenue. Agreements that imply they can create liability for things outside the agency's control.

Mistake

No ad-spend authorization

Running client budget without a defined approval threshold is a major risk. Define the max monthly spend the agency can commit without explicit client approval.

Mistake

Agency owns the accounts

Ad accounts, social profiles, and analytics should always be in the client's name. Agencies who own the accounts hold the client hostage on termination.

Mistake

No content approval deadline

When a client takes three weeks to approve a post that was due yesterday, the agency misses the timeline. A deemed-approved clause prevents this.

When to ask a lawyer

Where AI ends, counsel begins.

BizDraft is a drafting tool. It is not a substitute for a lawyer who knows your specific situation. Reach for one when:

Performance-based compensation

When any portion of the fee is tied to revenue, conversions, or other outcomes, the measurement methodology and dispute resolution process warrant attorney drafting.

Large ad budgets

When managing over $100,000/month in ad spend, liability for unauthorized spend or platform errors can be significant. Attorney review of the indemnification clauses is warranted.

Regulated advertising categories

Healthcare, financial products, and alcohol advertising have regulatory compliance requirements that affect what the agency can and cannot do. Legal review before signing is important.

FAQ

Marketing agreement questions.

Can a marketing agency guarantee results in a contract?

Be very careful. Guaranteeing specific revenue, traffic, or ranking outcomes creates liability for factors outside your control — algorithm changes, competitor spend, client-side conversion problems. Use best-efforts language and define KPIs as targets, not guarantees.

Who should own the Google Ads or Facebook ad account?

The client should always own their own ad accounts. The agency gets admin access to manage them. Accounts in the agency's name are held hostage — if the relationship ends, the client loses their data, history, and audience lists.

What happens to campaigns when the contract ends?

The agreement should specify a handover process — the agency provides all account credentials, campaign files, analytics access, and documentation within a defined number of business days after the final invoice is paid.

Can the agency use the client's campaign results in their portfolio?

Only if the agreement allows it. Many clients want confidentiality on revenue numbers and campaign data. The BizDraft template includes a portfolio rights clause that can be switched on or off based on your negotiation.
