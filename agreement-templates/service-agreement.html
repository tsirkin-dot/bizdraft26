What a service agreement is for

One contract. Every engagement.

A service agreement defines exactly what you will do, what the client will pay, who owns the work product, and what happens when either side needs to walk away. It is the foundational document for any business providing services to another party.

Who uses it

Agencies, consultants, designers, developers, coaches, accountants, and any business that delivers a service and invoices a client. If you do work for pay, you need this contract.

When to use it

Before work starts. Before the first invoice. Before the kickoff call. A signed service agreement sets expectations on both sides and is your first line of defense when a client disputes scope or payment.

When not to use it

Not a substitute for an employment agreement when the worker is actually your employee, and not ideal when you need a master agreement covering dozens of future projects — use an MSA for that.

Common mistakes

Where service agreements break down.

The most frequent reasons a service agreement fails to protect the provider or the client.

Mistake

Vague scope

Scope creep starts the moment a client can read ambiguity into what was agreed. Every deliverable should be named specifically.

Mistake

No change-order process

Without a written process for scope changes, every new request is free work. Define how extras get priced and approved.

Mistake

IP ownership left silent

If the agreement is silent on IP, ownership rules vary by state and work type. Silence benefits neither party — state it explicitly.

Mistake

No late-payment mechanism

Without late fees or suspension rights, a client can pay 90 days late with no consequence. Include both.

Mistake

Unlimited liability exposure

Without a liability cap, one bad project could expose the provider to losses far exceeding the contract value.

When to ask a lawyer

Where AI ends, counsel begins.

BizDraft is a drafting tool. It is not a substitute for a lawyer who knows your specific situation. Reach for one when:

High-value contract

Contracts above $50,000 or with enterprise clients who have their own legal review process warrant attorney involvement on your side too.

Regulated industries

Healthcare, financial services, legal services, or government contracts carry sector-specific compliance requirements a template cannot anticipate.

International clients

Cross-border service agreements need consideration for choice-of-law, currency, VAT, and jurisdiction enforcement that local counsel should address.

FAQ

Service agreement questions.

What is the difference between a service agreement and a contract?

No legal difference — a service agreement is a contract. The term "service agreement" signals the subject matter: one party provides services to another. Both words mean the same thing in court.

Does a service agreement need to be notarized?

No. Most service agreements are binding with just the parties' signatures — electronic or wet ink. Notarization is not required for service contracts in any US state.

Can I use one service agreement for all my clients?

A master template works well for standard engagements. Fill in the scope, fee, and duration for each client. For significantly different work types or client sizes, generate a new version — BizDraft is free every time.

What happens if the client wants to use their own contract?

You can negotiate. Having your own BizDraft draft gives you a baseline to compare against the client's paper and spot gaps — especially on IP ownership, liability limits, and payment terms.
