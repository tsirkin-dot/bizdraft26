What a retainer agreement is for

Predictable monthly revenue. Clear monthly commitment.

A retainer agreement establishes an ongoing monthly relationship between a service provider and a client. It defines the monthly fee, what is included, what happens to unused time, how scope changes are handled, and how either side exits the relationship.

Who uses it

Agencies, consultants, lawyers, accountants, PR firms, and any service provider who wants predictable recurring revenue in exchange for guaranteed monthly availability.

When to use it

When a client needs ongoing support rather than a one-time project. When you want to reserve capacity for a client each month and need to be compensated for that availability.

When not to use it

Not ideal for one-off projects with a defined end date — use a project-based service agreement instead. Retainers work best when the client genuinely has ongoing, recurring needs.

Common mistakes

Where retainer agreements create problems.

The most common reasons retainer relationships turn into resentment.

Mistake

No hour cap

A retainer without an hour limit can consume your entire month for a single client. Always define the monthly hour allocation explicitly.

Mistake

Ambiguous rollover policy

If the agreement is silent on rollover, a client can demand banked hours from six months ago. State the policy clearly — expire or carry forward, with a defined limit.

Mistake

No minimum commitment

Without a minimum term, clients can cancel month-to-month. If you've turned down other work to keep the retainer client, you need a minimum commitment period.

Mistake

Vague scope definition

"Ongoing support" is not a scope. Define exactly what activities are included so you can say no to out-of-scope requests without a fight.

Mistake

No annual rate adjustment

Long-term retainers without rate escalation clauses lock you into yesterday's price. Include an annual adjustment provision.

When to ask a lawyer

Where AI ends, counsel begins.

BizDraft is a drafting tool. It is not a substitute for a lawyer who knows your specific situation. Reach for one when:

Legal retainers

Attorney retainer agreements have specific ethics rules and trust accounting requirements that differ from standard service retainers. Use legal-specific forms.

High-value, long-term engagements

Retainers over $10,000/month or with multi-year minimums warrant attorney review, particularly around termination rights and liability caps.

Enterprise clients

When the client is a large company with their own legal team, expect redlines and get support reviewing their changes to your standard terms.

FAQ

Retainer agreement questions.

Is a retainer an advance payment or a fee for availability?

Both models exist. A "fee for availability" retainer pays the provider to reserve capacity regardless of how much work is actually used — unused hours don't roll over. An "advance payment" retainer is a deposit applied against actual hours worked. BizDraft asks which model you want and drafts accordingly.

What happens if the client goes over the included hours?

You bill at the agreed overage rate — typically your hourly rate or a negotiated premium. The agreement should specify whether you need pre-approval before exceeding the included hours or whether you bill overages automatically.

Can a client cancel a retainer at any time?

Only if the agreement lets them. Include a minimum commitment period (typically 3–6 months) and a notice requirement (typically 30 days). This protects you from clients who sign and cancel as soon as the first deliverable is done.

Should unused hours roll over?

It depends on your model. Rollover benefits the client but creates planning challenges for you. Most service retainers use a "use it or lose it" policy. If you offer rollover, cap it at one month's accumulation to limit exposure.
