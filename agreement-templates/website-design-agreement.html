What a website design agreement is for

Design it. Launch it. Hand it over cleanly.

A website design agreement defines everything from the first wireframe to the post-launch handoff. It covers revision rounds, who provides content and when, IP ownership of design assets, the launch checklist, and what happens after launch during the transition period.

Who uses it

Web designers, web design agencies, UX/UI designers, and digital studios creating websites, landing pages, or web applications for clients on a project basis.

When to use it

Before the discovery call turns into a brief. Before wireframes are shared. Before the deposit is taken. A signed agreement before any design work begins sets boundaries on revisions, content timelines, and what "done" looks like.

When not to use it

If the engagement includes significant custom development alongside design, use a software development agreement or add a dev scope section. Pure website design with a theme or no-code builder is the intended use case.

Common mistakes

Where website projects go sideways.

The most common reasons web design projects run over budget, over time, and under-paid.

Mistake

Unlimited revisions

The phrase "unlimited revisions" in a proposal turns a fixed-price project into an open-ended time sink. Cap revisions, define what a round means, and charge for overages.

Mistake

No content deadline

Web design cannot proceed without client content. If the client delivers copy six weeks late, the designer gets blamed for a delayed launch. Set a firm content deadline with automatic timeline extension.

Mistake

Holding source files hostage

Source files (Figma, Photoshop, etc.) must transfer to the client on final payment. If the agreement is silent, disputes over "we need the source files" are common.

Mistake

No third-party cost disclosure

Fonts, plugins, stock photos, and themes have ongoing licensing costs. If the client doesn't know about them at signing, the designer gets blamed when they expire.

When to ask a lawyer

Where AI ends, counsel begins.

BizDraft is a drafting tool. It is not a substitute for a lawyer who knows your specific situation. Reach for one when:

E-commerce with complex custom dev

Large Shopify builds with custom checkout, integrations, or PCI-compliance requirements go beyond standard web design and may need legal review of the liability provisions.

Enterprise clients

When a Fortune 500 or large enterprise has their own legal team and procurement process, having an attorney review their standard web design terms before you sign protects you.

ADA accessibility requirements

If the client requires WCAG compliance or ADA accessibility warranties, the scope, testing methodology, and liability for non-compliance should be reviewed by counsel.

FAQ

Website design agreement questions.

What counts as a revision round?

A revision round is one consolidated set of feedback submitted in a single document or session. Multiple back-and-forth messages should not count as separate rounds. Define a round as: the client submits one batch of feedback, the designer implements it, and the client reviews again. That is one round.

Does the client get the Figma or source files?

Only if your agreement says so. The BizDraft default includes source file transfer on final payment. If you prefer to retain source files and only hand over the exported assets, you can change that in the template before sending.

What if the client provides bad-quality images or wrong copy?

The designer is not responsible for the quality or accuracy of client-provided content. The agreement should state that the designer will use client-provided content as-is and that the client warrants they have the rights to use all content they supply.

Is post-launch support included?

Only if you include it. A time-limited post-launch support period (e.g., 14 days for launch-related bug fixes) is common and reasonable. Ongoing support beyond that should be a separate retainer agreement.
