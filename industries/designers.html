What makes designer contracts different

Design is both a service and a product. Contracts need to cover both.

A design contract isn't just a services agreement. It has to answer: who owns the files, when, in what format, and what the client can do with them. UX/UI work adds questions about design systems, component libraries, and handoff formats.

Industry-specific clauses

IP transfer on payment. Revision count with change-order rate. Design-system and source-file ownership. Portfolio display rights. Font and asset licensing pass-through. The clauses that protect a designer's real deliverables.

Common deal types

Brand identity package, UX audit, ongoing product design, agency subcontract, white-label work. Each structure has different IP, delivery, and termination needs.

Common deal types

Five shapes a design engagement takes.

Brand identity

Logo & brand system

Fixed deliverable set: logo files, color palette, typography, brand guide. Design services agreement with milestone payments and IP transfer at final delivery.

UX/UI project

Product design — research through handoff

UX research, wireframes, high-fidelity mockups, design system, Figma source. Design services agreement with explicit source-file and design-system ownership terms.

Ongoing design

Product or marketing retainer

Regular design output — features, assets, campaigns. Retainer agreement with monthly hour bank and IP transfer rolling with each deliverable batch.

Agency work

White-label or subcontract

Working under an agency on their client projects. Subcontractor agreement with flow-down IP, confidentiality, and non-solicitation of the agency's clients.

Asset sale

Template or UI kit license

Selling or licensing design templates, icon sets, or UI kits. License agreement with use-case restrictions, no-redistribution clause, and support terms.

The designer risk map

Five risks designers face. All contractable.

Unpaid IP transfer

Client publishes the logo before paying the final invoice. Fix: IP transfer clause conditioned on receipt of full payment. Until paid, you own the work.

Endless revisions

"Just one more round" — six rounds later. Fix: revision limit in the SOW with a defined change-order rate for anything beyond it.

Source-file dispute

Client demands the Figma source or native files that weren't part of the agreed deliverable. Fix: explicit source-file delivery clause specifying exactly what files are included.

Portfolio restriction

Client tries to prevent you from showing the work. Fix: portfolio display rights clause reserving your right to show the project, subject to NDA carve-outs.

Scope drift

Brand identity becomes a full website becomes a trade show booth. Fix: clear scope definition and a change-order process that documents and prices expansions.
