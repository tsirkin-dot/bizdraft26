What makes consulting contracts different

Consulting sells judgment, not just time. The contract has to draw the line between advice and accountability.

Consulting agreements have to define what you're advising on, what you're not, what you'll deliver, and — critically — what the client can't hold you responsible for when they act on your recommendations. The scope and liability clauses are the most important sections.

Industry-specific clauses

Scope of advisory services. Deliverable specification (reports, roadmaps, recommendations). Conflict-of-interest disclosure. Client authority to act on advice. Liability limitation. Non-solicitation of client staff. Retainer vs. project billing structures.

Common deal types

Strategic advisory retainer, project-based consulting, interim executive placement, expert witness, board advisory, and fractional leadership. Each structure has different scope, payment, and liability terms.

Common deal types

Five shapes a consulting engagement takes.

Retainer

Ongoing strategic advisory

Monthly access to your expertise — calls, reviews, recommendations. Advisory retainer with defined availability, monthly fee, and 30-day notice.

Project

Fixed-scope consulting project

Market entry strategy, operational review, process redesign. Consulting services agreement with SOW, milestone payments, and deliverable acceptance criteria.

Fractional

Fractional executive (CMO, COO, CFO)

Part-time leadership role. Defined days per month, decision-making authority scope, and confidentiality of strategic information.

Hourly

Time-and-materials advisory

Open-ended consulting at an hourly rate. Monthly invoicing, expense reimbursement, and maximum hours cap to control client spend.

Board / expert

Board advisory or expert witness

Equity, cash, or fee-for-service advisory arrangements. Board seat agreements or expert engagement letters with defined scope and compensation.

The consulting risk map

Five consulting risks. All preventable in the agreement.

Outcome liability

Client acts on your recommendation, it doesn't work, and they blame you. Fix: advice-is-advisory clause and liability cap — your liability is capped at fees paid for the engagement.

Scope bleed

A strategy engagement becomes execution becomes implementation. Fix: precise scope-of-services definition with a change-order process for anything beyond the original brief.

Conflict of interest

You're advising two clients who turn out to be competitors. Fix: conflict-of-interest disclosure and acknowledgment clause — client knows your existing roster; you disclose new conflicts as they arise.

IP ownership of work product

Who owns the strategy document, roadmap, or framework you developed? Fix: work-product ownership clause — typically client-owned for bespoke deliverables, but methodologies and templates remain yours.

Staff poaching

Client hires your staff or sub-contractors directly after working with them on a project. Fix: non-solicitation clause covering your team for a defined period post-engagement.
