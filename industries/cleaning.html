What makes cleaning contracts different

Cleaners have unsupervised access to private property. The contract has to address that trust — and limit it precisely.

Cleaning service agreements have unique needs: property access authorization (key codes, physical keys, or supervised entry), liability for breakage or damage during cleaning, what's included in a standard clean versus extras, and how the recurring service renews and terminates.

Industry-specific clauses

Property access authorization and key-holding terms. Liability cap for accidental breakage. Included vs. add-on services. Supplies provided by which party. Late-cancellation fee. Background check acknowledgment. Confidentiality for client home or business.

Common deal types

Weekly or bi-weekly recurring residential cleaning, one-time deep clean, move-in/move-out cleaning, commercial office cleaning, short-term rental turnover cleaning, and post-construction cleaning.

The cleaning risk map

Five cleaning risks. All preventable in the contract.

Breakage and damage

You accidentally break something during a clean. Fix: liability cap clause limiting your responsibility to a defined amount (e.g., cost of item or a fixed cap), with insurance backing above that threshold.

Missing item accusations

Client claims something went missing after your visit. Fix: pre-service walkthrough documentation clause — note existing damage and valuables before starting the clean.

Last-minute cancellation

Client cancels the morning of a scheduled clean. You blocked the time, bought supplies. Fix: late-cancellation fee for notice received within 24–48 hours.

Key or access abuse

Ambiguity around who has the key and when entry is permitted. Fix: explicit access authorization clause with key-holding terms, permitted entry windows, and return-of-key requirement on termination.

Scope dispute

"I thought windows were included." Fix: detailed scope-per-visit checklist in the agreement, with add-on services priced separately.
