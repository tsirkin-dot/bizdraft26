What makes retail contracts different

Small sellers deal with suppliers, fulfillment partners, and return policies — each of which needs its own written terms.

Retail and e-commerce businesses need agreements on both sides: with the vendors supplying the products and with the fulfillment or logistics partners shipping them. Each relationship has different payment, liability, and performance terms that generic templates don't capture.

Industry-specific clauses

Vendor pricing and minimum order quantity. Exclusivity and territory restrictions. Fulfillment SLA — shipping time, accuracy, and error handling. Returns and restocking fee terms. Data sharing and customer information handling. Brand approval for product representations.

Common deal types

Wholesale vendor agreement, dropshipping arrangement, 3PL fulfillment agreement, consignment agreement, reseller agreement, and white-label product deal.

Common deal types

Five shapes a retail business relationship takes.

Wholesale

Buy-resell wholesale arrangement

You buy at wholesale, sell at retail. Vendor agreement with pricing, MOQ, lead times, quality standards, and IP warranty from the supplier.

Dropship

Dropship with direct fulfillment

Supplier ships direct to customer. Dropshipping agreement with fulfillment SLA, shipping error handling, and customer return process.

3PL

Third-party logistics

Outsourcing storage and shipping to a 3PL. Fulfillment agreement with SLA, inventory accuracy, damage liability, and transition-out terms.

Private label

White-label product agreement

Manufacturer produces under your brand. IP ownership of brand on product, quality standards, exclusivity, and minimum purchase commitments.

Marketplace

Marketplace seller agreement

Selling on a third-party marketplace. Supplier terms for marketplace use, MAP pricing compliance, brand protection, and returns handling.

The retail risk map

Five retail risks. All preventable in the contract.

Supplier IP risk

Supplier doesn't actually own what they're selling you. Fix: IP warranty clause — supplier represents and warrants they have the right to sell and that the product doesn't infringe third-party rights.

Fulfillment errors

3PL ships wrong items or damages goods. Fix: SLA with error-rate tolerance, damage liability, and remedy — re-ship, refund, or credit — built into the fulfillment agreement.

Returns liability

Who bears the cost of returned goods — you or the supplier? Fix: returns clause in the vendor agreement defining who pays for return shipping, restocking, and damaged returns.

Price change ambiguity

Supplier raises prices mid-relationship. Fix: price change notice clause requiring X days' written notice before any price increase applies to new orders.

Exclusivity violation

You believe you have exclusive territory; supplier sells to a competitor in your market. Fix: explicit exclusivity definition with territory, channel, and duration — and remedies for breach.
