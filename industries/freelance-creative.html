What makes creative contracts different

Creative work has IP at the center. Generic templates miss that.

A graphic designer's contract isn't a generic services agreement with a logo on it. The core legal questions — who owns the work, when does ownership transfer, what can the client do with it — are specific to creative engagements.

Industry-specific clauses

IP transfer triggered by full payment. Revision limits with change-order triggers. Model release and usage-rights language. Portfolio display rights. These are the clauses generic templates omit.

Common deal types

One-time projects, recurring retainers, licensing deals, event-day engagements, content libraries. Each structure needs different payment, IP, and termination terms.

The freelance creative risk map

Five risks. All preventable in the contract.

IP ownership risk

Client uses the deliverable before paying. Fix: IP transfer clause triggered by receipt of full payment — ownership doesn't pass until payment clears.

Scope creep risk

"Can you just tweak this one more time?" Fix: revision limit with a defined change-order process and additional-hour rate for anything beyond it.

Approval delay risk

Client goes quiet and the project stalls. Fix: approval-timeout clause — if no feedback within X days, deliverable is deemed accepted.

Usage overreach risk

Client uses work for purposes beyond the agreed license. Fix: explicit usage-rights clause defining platform, duration, and territory.

Kill-fee risk

Client cancels at 70% complete. Fix: kill-fee clause that compensates for work done at termination, based on project completion percentage.
