What makes health and medical contracts different

Health and medical work sits at the intersection of service delivery, data sensitivity, and regulatory classification.

A health and medical contract has to handle three distinct risk areas: scope of services (what the practitioner or contractor is actually providing), data handling (how patient and health information is treated), and worker classification (independent contractor vs employee distinctions that carry significant compliance implications). Generic service templates handle one of the three — and usually miss the data and classification issues that matter most in this industry.

Industry-specific clauses

Scope-of-services definitions, HIPAA-awareness acknowledgments, patient data handling obligations, independent contractor classification representations, credentialing and licensing warranties, fee and billing arrangements, term and termination rights, and non-solicitation of patients. These are the clauses that protect health and medical professionals specifically.

Common engagement types

Locum tenens arrangements, independent contractor physician agreements, allied health professional service contracts, wellness provider agreements, medical staffing arrangements, and practice management service contracts. Each has different classification, liability, and data-handling structures.

Common deal types

Six shapes a health and medical engagement takes.

Locum tenens

Temporary physician coverage

Short-term clinical coverage for a practice or facility. Independent contractor agreement with defined coverage period, call schedule, credentialing obligations, malpractice insurance allocation, and patient record handling terms.

Allied health

Allied health professional contract

For nurse practitioners, physician assistants, therapists, and other allied health professionals engaged by a practice or health system. Scope of clinical authority, supervision requirements, compensation, and term.

Wellness

Wellness and coaching services

Health coaching, nutrition counseling, fitness or rehabilitation services. Session-based or retainer structure, informed consent acknowledgments, scope limitations relative to licensed medical practice, and cancellation policy.

Medical staffing

Medical staffing arrangement

Placement of clinical staff — nurses, technicians, administrative personnel — into a practice or facility. Staffing service agreement with placement terms, credentialing verification obligations, and fee structure.

Practice management

Practice management services contract

Administrative or operational services for a medical practice — billing, scheduling, compliance support. Service scope, data access obligations, confidentiality terms, and performance metrics.

Retainer

Ongoing clinical or advisory retainer

Regular retained engagement for ongoing clinical advisory, medical direction, or wellness program services. Monthly retainer amount, defined availability and deliverables, and renewal or termination terms.

The health and medical risk map

Five risks health and medical professionals face on every engagement.

Worker misclassification

A physician or practitioner treated as an employee without the proper classification documentation. Fix: independent contractor agreement with explicit autonomy, scheduling, and equipment-ownership terms that reflect and support the actual working relationship.

Scope of services disputes

Practice or client expects services beyond what was agreed — additional call coverage, administrative duties, or patient volume. Fix: detailed scope-of-services clause specifying hours, patient volume expectations, and what falls outside the engagement.

Patient data exposure

Contractor accesses patient information without adequate confidentiality obligations in place. Fix: confidentiality clause acknowledging the sensitivity of health information, restricting use and disclosure, and addressing obligations that survive contract termination.

Malpractice coverage gaps

Locum or contractor performs clinical work without clear agreement on who carries malpractice coverage. Fix: explicit insurance-obligation clause specifying which party maintains coverage, minimum limits, and tail coverage responsibilities on termination.

Patient solicitation after termination

Departing contractor continues treating patients they served under the engagement, redirecting them to a competing practice. Fix: non-solicitation clause with defined scope and duration, balanced against applicable state law restrictions on patient-access rights.
