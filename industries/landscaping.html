What makes landscaping contracts different

Landscaping is seasonal, weather-dependent, and scope often changes once you're on-site. The contract has to account for all three.

A landscaping contract needs to handle the recurring nature of maintenance work, the way weather and growing conditions change the scope of each visit, and the discovery of unexpected work (buried irrigation, diseased trees, drainage problems) that wasn't visible from the initial quote.

Industry-specific clauses

Seasonal service schedule. Weather-delay policy. Change-order trigger for discovered conditions. Plants and materials pricing (allowance vs. fixed). Property access authorization. Equipment damage liability. Callout fee for unscheduled visits.

Common deal types

Weekly mowing and maintenance, seasonal cleanup, landscaping installation project, irrigation system, tree service, snow removal, and commercial property contracts.

The landscaping risk map

Five landscaping risks. All preventable in the contract.

Hidden-condition liability

You hit an irrigation line or underground obstacle not disclosed by the client. Fix: client disclosure requirement for underground utilities and a liability clause for undisclosed conditions.

Scope expansion

"While you're here, can you also..." Fix: change-order clause requiring written authorization before any work outside the original scope begins.

Weather and seasonal disputes

Client expects a visit during a week of rain. Fix: weather-delay policy defining conditions under which visits are skipped or rescheduled without penalty.

Materials cost

Plants and materials cost more than the quote. Fix: materials pricing clause — either fixed-price (cost risk on you) or allowance-based (client approves overages above the threshold).

Mid-season cancellation

Client cancels a season contract at week four of twenty. Fix: cancellation notice requirement with work-to-date payment and minimum-term provision for seasonal contracts.
