What makes photography contracts different

Photography sits at the intersection of service, IP, and likeness rights.

A photography contract has to handle three distinct legal areas: service delivery (the shoot), intellectual property (who owns the images), and likeness rights (model and property releases). Generic service templates handle one of the three.

Industry-specific clauses

Model release language, property release, usage rights by platform and duration, raw-file delivery terms, date-lock and cancellation fee, overtime rate, second-shooter terms. These are the clauses that protect photographers specifically.

Common deal types

Commercial advertising, portrait sessions, wedding and event coverage, editorial licensing, stock photography, and agency subcontracting. Each has different IP, delivery, and cancellation structures.

Main contract

Photography services agreement

Shoot scope, deliverable count, delivery timeline, payment and deposit, usage rights, cancellation terms, and liability. The document every shoot needs.

Likeness consent

Model release

Written consent to use a subject's likeness in commercial, editorial, or advertising contexts. Required for any published image featuring an identifiable person.

Location consent

Property release

Permission to photograph and commercially use images of a private property, interior, or identifiable location. Required for advertising and some editorial uses.

Image licensing

Photo license agreement

License existing images for specific uses — advertising, editorial, social, print — with defined platform, duration, territory, and exclusivity terms.

Second shooter

Subcontractor agreement

For bringing in a second photographer on large events. Flow-down IP terms, confidentiality, and rate terms.

Pre-shoot

NDA

For pre-launch product shoots or confidential brand work where the client needs assurance before sharing brief materials.

Common deal types

Six shapes a photography engagement takes.

Commercial

Advertising & product photography

Usage rights are the main deliverable. Photography services agreement with explicit usage scope: platform, duration, territory, exclusivity. IP typically licensed, not assigned.

Portrait

Portrait session

Headshots, family portraits, personal branding. Defined image count, print or digital delivery, limited usage rights for personal use.

Event

Wedding & event coverage

Date-locked contract with deposit, cancellation tiers, overtime rate, delivery timeline, and second-shooter terms. One of the highest-stakes photo contracts to get right.

Editorial

Magazine & media licensing

One-time or multi-use editorial license. Defined publication, issue, and territory. Moral rights and credit-line requirements included.

Stock

Royalty-free or rights-managed license

Licensing existing images with defined use restrictions. Royalty-free (unlimited use within scope) vs rights-managed (per-use fee) structures.

Retainer

Monthly content retainer

Regular shoots for a brand's social and marketing needs. Monthly shoot quota, deliverable count, usage rights rolling with each batch.

The photographer risk map

Five risks photographers face on every job.

Usage overreach

Client uses images for advertising after licensing them for social only. Fix: explicit usage-rights clause with platform, duration, territory, and a fee schedule for expanded use.

Last-minute cancellation

Client cancels the day before a booked event. That date is now unbookable. Fix: non-refundable deposit and tiered cancellation fee based on days before the shoot.

Likeness dispute

Publishing an image of an identifiable person without consent. Fix: model release signed at the shoot — before the images are delivered or published.

Raw-file demand

Client demands all RAW files — which weren't included in the price. Fix: explicit clause specifying exactly what files are delivered and in what format.

Credit and attribution

Client publishes images with no photographer credit. Fix: credit-line requirement clause specifying how and where attribution must appear.
