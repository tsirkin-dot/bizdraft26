What makes coaching contracts different

Coaching involves advice, guidance, and in some cases physical activity. The contract has to limit liability and define exactly what kind of professional you are.

Coaching agreements need to address: what you're providing (coaching, not therapy; training, not medical advice), what the client is responsible for, liability waivers for physical activity, refund policy, and confidentiality of coaching conversations.

Industry-specific clauses

Coaching-is-not-therapy disclaimer. Fitness advice is not medical advice disclaimer. Physical activity liability waiver. Refund policy for prepaid packages. Confidentiality of session content. Scope of sessions (frequency, format, duration). Results-not-guaranteed disclaimer.

Common deal types

1-on-1 coaching engagement, group coaching program, online course with coaching, fitness training package, corporate wellness training, and executive coaching retainer.

The coaching risk map

Five coaching risks. All preventable in the agreement.

Results claim liability

Client expected guaranteed outcomes and is demanding a refund when they don't materialize. Fix: results-not-guaranteed disclaimer and refund policy that's clear before enrollment.

Physical injury

Client is injured during a training session. Fix: liability waiver signed before first physical session — client acknowledges inherent risk and releases you from injury claims caused by their own participation.

Professional scope confusion

Client treats your coaching as therapy or your fitness advice as medical advice. Fix: explicit disclaimer — coaching is not therapy; fitness guidance is not medical advice. Client consults their own professionals.

Package refund dispute

Client wants a full refund after completing half the sessions. Fix: clear refund policy in the package agreement — typically pro-rated for unused sessions or no-refund for cohort programs.

Confidentiality breach

Coaching session content shared inappropriately. Fix: confidentiality clause covering all session content — what you discuss stays confidential, with defined exceptions (safety risk, legal obligation).
