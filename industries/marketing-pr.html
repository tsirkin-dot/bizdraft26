What makes marketing contracts different

Marketing work crosses IP, performance expectations, and client approval loops. The contract has to handle all three.

Marketing and PR contracts have to address content ownership (who owns the ads, copy, and creative), the approvals process (who approves what before it goes live), performance disclaimers (you're not guaranteeing results), and how the client's brand and confidential strategy are protected.

Industry-specific clauses

Content ownership and IP transfer. Client approvals process with deemed-approval timeout. Performance disclaimer — results not guaranteed. Ad spend handling (who controls the accounts). NDA for client roadmap, pricing, and customer data. Non-solicitation of the client's client list.

Common deal types

Monthly marketing retainer, campaign-based engagement, content production contract, PR retainer, paid media management, influencer campaign, and white-label agency work.

The marketing risk map

Five marketing risks. All preventable in the contract.

Results guarantee claim

Client expects a specific ROI or traffic number. Fix: performance disclaimer clause stating that results depend on market conditions and client factors, not a guaranteed outcome.

Approval loop delay

Campaigns can't launch because client isn't approving assets. Fix: approval process clause with deemed-approval — if no response within X business days, content is approved to proceed.

Content ownership dispute

Who owns the ad creative, copy, and brand assets after the retainer ends? Fix: IP ownership clause specifying when and how content ownership transfers to the client.

Ad spend liability

Client claims you overspent or misallocated their ad budget. Fix: ad spend authorization clause — client owns and approves the accounts; you recommend but they authorize spend.

Confidentiality breach

Client's unreleased campaign or pricing leaked from your team. Fix: NDA clause extending to sub-contractors and staff with access to client materials.
