What makes video contracts different

Video production has layers: capture, edit, delivery, and licensing. Each layer needs terms.

A video production contract has to address the production day, the edit process, what files get delivered, who owns the footage, and what the client is licensed to do with the final cut. Generic templates cover one layer.

Industry-specific clauses

Revision rounds for rough and final cut. Raw footage ownership and retention. Music licensing responsibility. Day-rate and overtime terms. Delivery format specification. Broadcast vs. digital licensing tiers.

Common deal types

Brand video, social content series, event coverage, documentary, corporate training, wedding, edit-only, and agency subcontract. Each structure has different IP, revision, and delivery needs.

The videographer risk map

Five production risks. All preventable in the contract.

Raw footage dispute

Client demands all raw files after the project. Fix: raw footage ownership clause — specify that unedited footage remains yours unless explicitly included in the deliverable and priced accordingly.

Revision spiral

Rough cut to final cut through twelve rounds of changes. Fix: defined revision rounds for rough cut and final cut, with an hourly rate for edits beyond the limit.

Music licensing liability

Client adds copyrighted music to the brief. Fix: music licensing responsibility clause specifying who secures sync rights and who bears the cost.

Broadcast overreach

Client uses a social-licensed video in a TV broadcast campaign. Fix: usage tier clause defining what platforms are covered and a rate schedule for expanded use.

Day-of cancellation

Client cancels the day before a locked shoot date. Crew is booked, gear is rented. Fix: cancellation fee clause covering committed costs and lost day rate.
