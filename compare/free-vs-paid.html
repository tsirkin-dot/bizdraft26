What's behind the price tag

Free means something. Paid means something. Neither is what it appears.

Most "free" tools are free trials, free-with-ads, or free-with-upsell. Most "paid" tools charge for things of varying value. Understanding the model matters.

Common "free" models

Free trial: Free for 7-14 days, then requires a subscription to keep the document or continue using the tool.

Free tier with upsell: Basic document free; download, e-signature, or "advanced" clauses require payment.

Free with account / data: The product is free; your data (and email address for marketing) is the cost.

What paid buys

Attorney-drafted templates: A human lawyer wrote the template for a specific use case. Often the highest-quality option for that exact use case — if you use it repeatedly.

Subscription features: Storage, version history, e-signature, attorney access — features beyond the document itself.

Specialized industry templates: Some paid products are specifically designed for construction, healthcare, or film — industries with unique legal requirements.

The quality question

Price doesn't predict quality for contract tools. A $50 one-time template may be a generic document with a price tag. A free AI generator may produce a more tailored draft. The question to ask: was this built by people who understand what the agreement needs — or was it built to check a price point?

When to pay, when not to

Paid is worth it in some situations. In others, it isn't.

The answer depends on what the paid product actually provides — not on the price alone.

Don't pay

One-off project agreement

You need a service agreement for one project. A free AI generator produces a tailored draft in minutes. Paying $30-$50 for a template provides no additional value for a one-time need.

Don't pay

Standard agreement type

Freelance agreement, NDA, consulting contract — standard agreement types that a good free generator handles well. The paid version of a standard template typically offers no clause advantage over a free generated equivalent.

Consider paying

Attorney-drafted template for a specific industry

A construction attorney's template for a subcontractor agreement. A film lawyer's production services agreement. Industry-specific templates written by specialists who know the industry's unique exposure. Worth the one-time cost if you use the same structure repeatedly.

Consider paying

You need more than the document

If you need e-signature, cloud storage, attorney access, or platform features alongside the agreement, a paid subscription may bundle value beyond the document itself. Evaluate whether you actually use the bundled features.

Don't pay

Free trial that converts to subscription

If you only need one document and the "free" tool converts to a subscription after 7 days, the effective cost is a month's subscription for one document. A free generator with no subscription model is the better value.

Always check

What the "free" tool is actually collecting

Some free tools collect your email for a marketing funnel. Some store your document content to train models. BizDraft collects nothing beyond standard server logs — the draft content is never stored on our side. That's the privacy tradeoff to consider.

Honest verdict

When does paid actually buy something?

Paid buys something real when: (1) the template was drafted by an attorney who specializes in your industry, (2) you use the same structure repeatedly and amortize the cost across many engagements, or (3) you need subscription features (storage, e-sign, attorney access) beyond the document itself.

Paid buys a false sense of security when: the template is generic with a price tag, when it's the same content as a free equivalent with a payment wall, or when it comes from a subscription that auto-renews after a trial.
