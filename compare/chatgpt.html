Purpose-built vs blank prompt.

The fundamental difference

General-purpose AI vs. a finished product.

Both use large language models. The difference is everything that was built around the model.

What ChatGPT is

A general-purpose AI interface. You provide all the context through your prompt. The quality of the output depends entirely on how well you frame the request. Without domain-specific instruction, ChatGPT produces a generic contract that may miss key protective clauses — or invent ones that sound plausible but don't reflect actual law.

What BizDraft is

A purpose-built drafting tool. The intake is designed around what contracts actually need. The AI has been trained and instructed to include standard protective clauses (liability cap, IP assignment, termination, confidentiality). The output format is a downloadable agreement, not a chatbot response.

The prompt problem

To get a good contract from ChatGPT, you need to know what a good contract includes. If you already know that, the prompt is useful. If you don't — if you're the person who didn't know about IP assignment triggers or liability caps — ChatGPT will draft what you asked for, which may not be what you needed.

When each wins

Purpose-built wins for contracts. General wins for everything else.

Honest about both. ChatGPT is a remarkable tool — just not optimized for this specific job.

BizDraft wins

You don't know what to ask for

BizDraft's intake is designed to prompt the right information. You describe the deal; the tool knows what questions to resolve. With ChatGPT, you're responsible for asking for everything — and for knowing what "everything" is.

BizDraft wins

Output format matters

BizDraft produces a formatted Word document ready to review, edit, and send. ChatGPT produces text in a chat window. Getting that into a proper contract document requires additional steps and formatting work.

BizDraft wins

Consistent protective clause coverage

BizDraft's defaults include standard protective clauses regardless of whether the user thought to ask. ChatGPT only includes what the user prompted. Users who don't know to ask for a liability cap won't get one.

ChatGPT wins

You're an expert who knows exactly what to ask

If you're a lawyer or experienced contract negotiator who knows precisely what clauses you want, ChatGPT's flexibility is valuable. You can iterate in real time, request specific language, and refine clause by clause.

ChatGPT wins

Clause-level editing and iteration

For refining a specific clause — "make this more favorable to the provider," "rewrite this in plain English" — ChatGPT's conversational interface is more flexible than a single-output tool.

Know the risk

Hallucination risk is higher in ChatGPT for contracts

ChatGPT can confidently cite non-existent statutes, invent clause language that sounds legal but isn't, or produce IP provisions that misstate federal copyright rules. Purpose-built tools can still make errors — but the risk of plausible-sounding legal fabrication is higher with a general model responding to an ad-hoc prompt.

Honest verdict

Which should you actually use?

For drafting a business agreement, BizDraft is the better tool. Not because ChatGPT can't produce a contract — it can. But because a purpose-built tool knows what belongs in a contract, produces a formatted output ready to use, and includes protective clauses by default that most users won't know to ask ChatGPT for.

ChatGPT is useful if you already know contract law and want to iterate on specific language. It's a tool for people who know what they're building. BizDraft is for people who need the output, not the process.
