What's happening here

A brand wants your audience. You want to get paid and stay protected.

Brand deals are one of the most misunderstood contracts in the creator economy. They involve IP rights (who owns the content after you post it), FTC compliance obligations, exclusivity that can block your other deals, and payment timing that often doesn't favor the creator.

The situation

A brand reached out (or you pitched them) for a paid partnership. They want content — posts, videos, stories, reviews — in exchange for a fee or product compensation. The collaboration is commercial and it involves your personal brand, your content, and your audience.

Why it matters

Without a written agreement, the brand may claim perpetual rights to use your content in paid ads without additional compensation. They may require exclusivity without specifying for how long. They may change the deliverables after you've started. Payment terms may be vague or delayed. These are common brand-deal problems with easy contractual solutions.

What goes wrong without one

Your Instagram post becomes a Facebook ad without extra payment. A brand deal with a competitor blocks you from other work because you had no defined exclusivity window. You post content that doesn't include required FTC disclosure language and face regulatory exposure. Payment arrives 90 days after posting with no recourse for the delay.

The right agreement for this situation

The influencer/creator agreement — and the clauses that matter most.

A creator agreement is a specialized service agreement built around the unique dynamics of sponsored content: deliverables measured in posts and views, FTC compliance obligations, content approval processes, and usage rights that extend beyond the posting date.

Deliverables and approval

Content deliverables clause

Defines exactly what content will be created: number of posts, format (feed post, Reel, Story, YouTube video, blog), platform, minimum word count or video length, and required hashtags or mentions. Also defines the brand's approval rights — how many revisions, how long they have to approve, and what happens if they don't respond.

Content ownership

Usage rights clause

You own the content you create. The brand gets a license to use it — for a defined period, on defined channels, for defined purposes. "Whitelist" and "dark posts" (using your content in paid ads) should be explicitly addressed and priced separately. Unlimited usage rights without additional compensation should be declined or negotiated.

FTC compliance

Disclosure obligation clause

FTC guidelines require clear disclosure of material connections in sponsored content. The agreement should specify both parties' obligations: the creator discloses the sponsorship using approved language (#ad, #sponsored, or equivalent), and the brand does not require the creator to post without disclosure. This protects both sides.

Competitive exclusivity

Exclusivity clause

Defines whether you're prohibited from working with competing brands, for how long (the exclusivity window), and what counts as a competitor. Exclusivity has real commercial cost — it should be priced accordingly. Exclusivity that isn't defined is effectively unlimited, which almost always favors the brand.

What BizDraft asks you

Six questions for a brand deal agreement.

1

Who are the parties?

Your name (or LLC name) and the brand or agency's legal name. If you work through a management company or MCN, that entity should be the contracting party.

2

What are the deliverables?

Number of posts, platforms, content format, required mentions, hashtags, and any minimum metrics. What is the posting schedule and timeline? How many rounds of revision does the brand get?

3

What are the usage rights?

How long can the brand use your content and on which channels? Is whitelisting or dark posting included? Is there an additional fee for extended usage rights? This clause has the most long-term financial impact.

4

What is the payment structure?

Flat fee, per-post rate, performance-based, or product-only. Payment timing — upon contract signing, upon posting, or net-30/60 after posting. Late payment terms. These are the most negotiated terms in brand deals.

5

Is there an exclusivity window?

How long are you prohibited from working with competing brands? What defines a competitor? Is the exclusivity bilateral (brand can't use competing creators) or one-sided? BizDraft flags if the exclusivity window is unusually broad.

6

What state governs?

Your state of residence or business formation. This governs contract enforcement, dispute resolution, and applicable consumer protection laws.

What to watch out for

Five brand deal red flags.

Unlimited usage rights for a flat fee

If the brand pays you once and gets perpetual rights to use your content in ads, you've sold usage rights that have compounding value for no additional compensation. Cap usage rights by time and channel — typically 6–12 months — or price unlimited rights significantly higher.

Vague or open-ended exclusivity

"Creator will not work with competing brands" without defining what a competitor is or how long the restriction lasts is effectively an unlimited exclusivity clause. Define the exclusivity window (typically 30–90 days for most deals), define what constitutes a competitor, and price the exclusivity separately.

Approval clause with no deadline

If the brand has unlimited time to approve your content, they can keep requesting revisions indefinitely. Set a specific approval window (5–7 business days) and specify that content is deemed approved if no feedback is received within that window.

No FTC disclosure provision

FTC guidelines require disclosure of paid partnerships. A brand that asks you not to disclose the sponsorship is asking you to violate FTC regulations — and you bear the enforcement risk, not them. Your agreement should require compliant disclosure and prohibit the brand from waiving this requirement.

Payment net-60 or net-90 after posting

Standard brand deal payment terms often favor the brand at the creator's expense. You create, you post, and then you wait two to three months for payment. Negotiate for 50% upfront or payment within 15–30 days of posting. At minimum, include a late payment provision with interest.

Frequently asked questions

Questions about brand deal agreements.

Does every brand deal need a written contract?

Any paid brand collaboration should have written terms. Even a simple email exchange confirming deliverables, payment, and usage rights is better than nothing. A proper agreement provides enforcement rights and creates a paper trail if anything goes wrong.

What is "whitelisting" and do I need to address it?

Whitelisting means giving the brand access to your social media account to run paid ads from your handle. It's significantly more valuable than organic posts and should be separately negotiated and priced. If your agreement doesn't mention whitelisting, the brand cannot do it — which is the correct default.

What if the brand's manager sends me their own contract?

Read the usage rights, exclusivity, and payment terms carefully. Usage rights that are "in perpetuity and worldwide" for all media are extremely broad — these effectively transfer your content to the brand forever. Counter-propose time-limited, channel-specific rights, and don't sign anything with unlimited exclusivity without defining the term and pricing it accordingly.

Am I required to disclose sponsorships by law?

Yes. The FTC requires clear and conspicuous disclosure of material connections in endorsements — including paid posts, gifted products, and affiliate relationships. #ad and #sponsored are widely accepted. "Partner" alone or burying the disclosure in a list of hashtags may not be sufficient. Your agreement should ensure both you and the brand understand these obligations.
