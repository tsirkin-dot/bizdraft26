What's happening here

You have a new client. Now you need a contract.

The verbal agreement is made. The excitement is real. The problem is that nothing has been written down yet — and what isn't written down will be disputed later.

The situation

A client hired you for a project, a service, or an ongoing engagement. They've agreed to the rate. Work begins soon. The relationship is new and the expectations are still unwritten.

Why it matters

Without a written agreement, "we agreed to X" becomes your word against theirs the moment anything changes. Scope expands. Payment slips. The client claims ownership of work you thought was yours.

What goes wrong without one

Scope creep with no recourse. Invoices that don't get paid. Deliverables used before payment clears. Projects that drag on with no clear end. All of these happen to professionals who rely on trust alone.

The right agreement for this situation

Three documents. Every new client engagement needs at least one.

The right combination depends on the complexity of the work, the client's size, and how sensitive the information being shared is.

Core contract

Service agreement

The primary document for any client engagement. Defines the relationship, payment terms, IP ownership, revision limits, termination, and governing law. Use this for any project where you're delivering a service.

Scope document

Statement of work (SOW)

The project-specific attachment. Fixed deliverables, defined milestones, hard deadlines. Works as a standalone for simple projects or as an exhibit to a master service agreement for repeat clients.

Pre-project privacy

NDA

If the client is sharing sensitive business information before or during the engagement — pricing strategy, customer lists, unreleased products — a mutual NDA protects both sides from the start.

For ongoing work

Retainer agreement

If this client will be paying you monthly for ongoing services — content, maintenance, advisory — a retainer agreement sets the fixed fee, hour limits, rollover rules, and cancellation notice.

What to watch out for

Four red flags in new-client agreements.

Vague scope definition

"Website redesign" means something different to everyone. If the contract doesn't list exactly what's included and excluded, the client's interpretation wins by default.

No deposit clause

Starting work without a deposit means you're financing the client's project. If they disappear at 60%, you have no recourse beyond small claims.

IP transfer before payment

If your contract transfers ownership on delivery rather than on full payment, the client can use your work and dispute the invoice simultaneously. Make IP transfer conditional on receipt of final payment.

No approval timeline

Contracts without approval deadlines let clients delay indefinitely. If a client doesn't respond within a defined window, work should be deemed approved and the project clock keeps moving.

Missing kill-fee clause

If a client cancels mid-project, what do they owe you? Without a termination clause specifying payment for completed work, you may walk away with nothing.

Frequently asked questions

Questions about new-client agreements.

Do I need both a service agreement and an SOW?

For a one-time project with a single client, a service agreement with scope built in is usually enough. For repeat clients or large projects, separating the master agreement from the project-specific SOW makes it easier to add new work without re-signing the whole contract.

What if the client wants to use their own contract instead?

Read theirs carefully — especially the IP clause. Large clients often use contracts that transfer all IP on execution, not on payment. Counter-propose payment-triggered transfer, and always check for non-compete clauses that limit your future work.

How do I handle a rush project with no time for paperwork?

BizDraft takes under five minutes. Send the draft digitally before you start the first task — even an email reply with "I agree" to the PDF creates a record. No signature is riskier than a fast signature.

Should I include a non-solicitation clause?

If you're a solo professional, a non-solicitation preventing the client from hiring your subcontractors or team members directly is worth including. If you're an agency, it's essential.
