What's happening here

Something went wrong. Now you're making sure it can't happen again.

The most common reason small business owners finally get serious about contracts isn't a lawyer's advice. It's a painful experience. An unpaid invoice. A client who used your work without paying. A partner who disappeared with the company's assets. The lesson sticks.

The situation

Something happened — a dispute, a loss, a betrayal — that could have been prevented or significantly mitigated with a written agreement. Now you're entering a new engagement and you won't make the same mistake. You need the right document, and you need it before anything starts.

Why it matters

Contracts don't prevent bad actors — they define the consequences. They create a paper trail that makes disputes resolvable. They establish expectations before misalignment happens. They're not just defensive documents; they're operating agreements that tell both parties exactly what was agreed to.

The lesson

It wasn't the other person being bad. It was the absence of a document that made the misalignment invisible until it was too late. Most business disputes happen between people who genuinely disagreed about what was agreed — not between people who intended harm from the start. Contracts prevent that ambiguity.

The right agreement for this situation

Find the right starting point for what happened — and what comes next.

The agreement you need depends on the type of engagement that went wrong. Here are the most common situations and the documents that address them.

"A client didn't pay me."

Service agreement with payment terms

Deposit upfront, payment milestones, late fees, and a suspension-of-work clause. IP transfers only on full payment. These four clauses address the most common non-payment scenarios. Start here.

"A client used my work without paying."

Service agreement with IP payment trigger

Under US copyright law, work created for hire belongs to the creator until ownership is formally transferred. A service agreement that ties IP transfer to receipt of full payment means the client using your work without paying is infringement — not just a billing dispute.

"A business partner walked away with the assets."

Partnership agreement with exit terms

Ownership, buyout mechanics, and what each partner can and cannot take on departure. An IP assignment to the business entity ensures the company — not an individual partner — owns the underlying assets.

"A contractor shared our confidential information."

NDA + IC agreement

A confidentiality obligation must exist before the information is shared. A contractor NDA creates a legally enforceable duty of secrecy and the right to seek injunctive relief and damages if the information is misused.

What to watch out for

Four mistakes people make the second time around.

Fixing the last problem but missing the next one

If a client didn't pay last time, you add a deposit clause — but skip the IP transfer trigger. If a partner took the assets, you add a buyout clause — but don't assign IP to the company. Address the specific problem, but draft the whole agreement.

Using a template designed for the other side

Contracts found online are often written from the other party's perspective. A client-side contract protects the client; a freelancer contract protects the freelancer. Make sure the template you're using is drafted from your position in the transaction.

Signing after the relationship starts

A contract signed three weeks into a project — or after money has already changed hands — is weaker than one signed before anything began. Courts scrutinize consideration in retroactive agreements. Get it signed before work starts.

Letting the relationship dilute the terms

You trust this person. They're a friend, a referral, someone from your network. The contract still matters. Disputes happen most often with people you trusted — because the absence of a contract is often how the trust was expressed.

Frequently asked questions

Questions from people who've been burned before.

Can I use a contract to retroactively fix a situation that's already gone wrong?

Retroactive agreements are enforceable if both parties sign with fresh consideration — but courts scrutinize them. If you're trying to document an existing arrangement, be explicit about the effective date and the consideration for signing now. A retroactive agreement is better than nothing, but not as strong as one signed before work started.

What's the most important clause to add when you've been burned on payment?

An IP transfer trigger tied to full payment. This converts a billing dispute into an IP dispute — a much stronger legal position. Until full payment is received, you own the work. The client using the work without paying is infringement, not just non-payment.

Will asking for a contract push away good clients?

Professional clients expect contracts. A client who resists signing a standard service agreement — not something unreasonable, just a professional agreement — is a client worth reconsidering. The discomfort of asking for a signature is far less than the discomfort of an unpaid invoice.

How do I bring up a contract with someone I already have a relationship with?

"I'm formalizing all my client agreements — here's what I'll be using going forward." Most people accept this framing without issue. If they push back on specific terms, that's a useful signal — and a negotiation worth having before the work starts.
