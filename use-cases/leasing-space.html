What's happening here

Someone is moving in, or you're moving in somewhere. Get it in writing.

Leases are among the most legally regulated agreements in the US. State and local law governs what must be disclosed, how much you can charge as a deposit, what grounds exist for eviction, and dozens of other terms. A lease that ignores these rules exposes you to liability — and may not be enforceable.

The situation

You're a landlord renting a residential unit, a tenant signing a commercial lease, or a business subleasing office space. The occupancy is happening; the paperwork needs to match the applicable state's requirements before move-in.

Why it matters

State landlord-tenant law imposes mandatory disclosures that, if omitted, can make the lease unenforceable or expose the landlord to statutory penalties. Deposit caps exist in most states — charging more than permitted creates liability. The lease is not just a preference document; it's a compliance document.

What goes wrong without one

Disputes over security deposit returns with no clear timeline or condition standard. Tenants who sublease without permission. Liability for lead paint or mold disclosures that should have been made. Eviction proceedings that fail because the lease didn't follow state procedure.

The right agreement for this situation

Three lease types. The right one depends on the space.

Residential, commercial, and sublease agreements have fundamentally different legal frameworks and clause requirements. Using the wrong template for your situation creates gaps that matter when a dispute arises.

Renting residential property

Residential lease agreement

For landlords renting apartments, houses, or condos. Covers rent amount, security deposit (state-capped), lease term, maintenance responsibilities, pet and smoking policies, late fees, renewal terms, and all state-mandated disclosures including lead paint, mold, and habitability.

Renting business space

Commercial lease agreement

For office, retail, or warehouse space. Covers base rent, common area maintenance (CAM) charges, use restrictions, buildout rights, assignment and subletting, exclusivity (retail), and personal guarantees. Commercial leases are negotiated, not statutory — terms vary far more than residential.

Subleasing space

Sublease agreement

When you're a tenant who needs to rent your space to another party. The sublease must be permitted by your primary lease, must not exceed the terms of the primary lease, and must clearly establish who the subtenant's obligations run to. Sublandlord liability to the master landlord continues.

Short-term use

Month-to-month rental agreement

For flexible arrangements where neither side wants a fixed term. Requires notice to terminate (typically 30 days, sometimes 60 depending on the state), and carries the same disclosure obligations as a fixed-term lease in most jurisdictions.

What to watch out for

Four lease red flags.

Deposit that exceeds the state cap

Most states cap security deposits at 1–2 months' rent. Charging more can result in the tenant recovering the excess plus penalties. BizDraft checks the cap for your state and flags if your deposit amount is over the limit.

Missing mandatory disclosures

Federal law requires lead paint disclosures for properties built before 1978. Many states require mold disclosures, bed bug history, landlord name and address, and more. Missing these doesn't just create a claim — in some states it creates a right for the tenant to break the lease.

Lease clause that violates tenant rights

Clauses that waive the implied warranty of habitability, prohibit tenants from calling inspectors, or impose fees not permitted by state law are void and unenforceable — and their inclusion can put the entire lease at risk.

No move-in condition documentation

The biggest source of security deposit disputes. Without a documented pre-move-in condition (photos, checklist), the tenant can claim every scratch was pre-existing. A move-in inspection addendum, referenced in the lease, closes this gap.

Sublease without master lease review

If your primary lease prohibits subletting, subleasing anyway creates grounds for eviction. Check your lease before subleasing. BizDraft's sublease agreement includes a representation that subletting is permitted by the master lease.

Frequently asked questions

Questions about lease agreements.

Does BizDraft cover all 50 states?

Yes. BizDraft generates state-adjusted leases for all 50 states. The deposit cap, required disclosures, notice period, and habitability language are all adjusted based on the property's state. For local ordinances (city-specific rent control, just-cause eviction), consult a local attorney.

Is a commercial lease different enough to matter?

Significantly. Commercial tenants have far fewer statutory protections than residential tenants — almost everything is negotiable. CAM charges, exclusivity clauses, assignment rights, and personal guarantees are commercial-only issues that a residential template won't address.

Can I use BizDraft's lease for a room rental?
