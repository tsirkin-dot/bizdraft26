What's happening here

You need to share something sensitive. Before the meeting.

The conversation can't happen without revealing something valuable. The question is whether you've created a legal obligation for the other party to keep it private before you open your mouth.

The situation

You have a meeting — with a potential partner, a vendor, an investor, or a potential acquirer. The conversation requires sharing details you don't share publicly: financials, product roadmap, client lists, proprietary processes, or trade secrets.

Why it matters

Without an NDA, the person across the table has no legal obligation to keep what you share confidential. They can walk away from the deal and use everything you told them. A signed NDA creates a legally enforceable duty of confidentiality before disclosure begins.

What goes wrong without one

A vendor who learns your pricing and shares it with a competitor. A potential investor who passes on your deal and builds the product themselves. A partner negotiation that falls through and leaves both sides knowing too much about each other. All of these happen every day without NDAs.

The right agreement for this situation

One-way or mutual — the right NDA for the right conversation.

The direction of information flow determines which type of NDA to use. Get it right before the meeting, not after.

You're sharing, they're receiving

One-way NDA (unilateral)

One party discloses, one party receives. The receiving party is bound by confidentiality; the disclosing party is not. Use this for investor pitches, vendor demos where you're evaluating a product, and situations where information flows in only one direction.

Both sides sharing

Mutual NDA (bilateral)

Both parties disclose and both are bound. Use this for partnership discussions, co-development conversations, M&A exploratory calls, and any situation where both sides will be sharing sensitive information about their businesses.

For multiple recipients

Multi-party NDA

When more than two parties are involved — a three-way partnership discussion or a pitch to multiple co-investors. A multi-party NDA covers all parties in a single document instead of requiring bilateral agreements with every combination.

What to watch out for

Four red flags in NDA drafting.

Vague confidentiality definition

An NDA that defines confidential information as "anything disclosed" is broad but weak in court. Stronger NDAs define the categories — financial data, customer information, technical specifications — and require marking or confirmation of confidentiality at disclosure.

Signing after the conversation

An NDA signed after information is already disclosed may not protect that information. The obligation must exist at the time of disclosure. Sign before the meeting, not at the end of it.

No permitted disclosure exceptions

A well-drafted NDA carves out information that becomes public independently, information the recipient already knew, or disclosures required by law. Without these carve-outs, you're creating obligations that can't be met and courts won't enforce.

Investors rarely sign NDAs

Most institutional investors — VCs, angels — won't sign an NDA before an initial pitch. It's standard practice. Focus on protecting your most sensitive information by not sharing it in early-stage conversations, rather than leading with an NDA demand that will end the conversation.

Frequently asked questions

Questions about NDAs.

Does an NDA actually prevent someone from misusing my information?

It creates a legal obligation and the right to sue for breach. It won't physically prevent misuse, but it means the person who violates it faces real legal consequences — damages, injunctions, and attorney's fees. That's a meaningful deterrent.

Should I use a mutual NDA even if I'm the one doing most of the sharing?

Mutual NDAs are often easier to get signed because they're perceived as fair. If you're sharing substantially more, a one-way NDA is more accurate. If the other party balks at a one-way NDA, a mutual is a reasonable compromise.

How long should an NDA last?

Two to three years is standard for most business NDAs. Trade secrets can be protected indefinitely. Very long NDAs (10+ years) are harder to enforce because courts question whether the information remains sensitive. BizDraft defaults to 3 years with a perpetual trade-secret carve-out.

What if someone refuses to sign?

It's a signal worth noting. A vendor or partner who won't sign an NDA may not be someone you should be sharing sensitive information with. For investor pitches, focus on sharing non-sensitive information until you're further in the process — or prepare a limited, version-controlled deck.

Is a free NDA from BizDraft legally binding?

A properly drafted NDA signed by both parties is legally binding regardless of what it cost to draft. The enforceability depends on the quality of the language and the circumstances of signing — not the price of the tool that generated it.
