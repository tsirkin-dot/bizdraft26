What's happening here

You're bringing in outside help. Two things are at risk.

You need work done that isn't worth a full-time hire. You found the right person. The problem is that everything you're about to give them access to — your systems, your strategy, your clients — has no legal protection without a signed agreement.

The situation

You've agreed on a project and a rate. The contractor is an independent professional, not an employee. The work begins soon. Nothing is in writing about who owns what they build or what happens to your business information.

Why it matters

Under US copyright law, work created by a contractor belongs to the contractor by default — not you. Without a written IP assignment, the developer who built your app may legally own it. The IRS also scrutinizes contractor classification, and misclassification carries back taxes and penalties.

What goes wrong without one

Contractors who walk away and take the code. Confidential client lists exposed to a competitor. An IRS audit reclassifying your contractor as an employee because the working relationship looked like employment. These are common, not edge cases.

The right agreement for this situation

Three documents. All of them matter when hiring outside help.

The IC agreement covers the working relationship. The NDA covers information. The IP assignment covers ownership. Together they close the gaps that expose businesses when contractors are involved.

Core contract

Independent contractor agreement

Defines the 1099 relationship explicitly. Establishes the contractor's independence (their own tools, their own hours, no benefits), the scope of work, payment terms, and termination rights. This is the classification firewall.

Confidentiality

NDA (one-way)

The contractor is receiving your confidential information — client names, pricing, source code, business strategy. A one-way NDA makes it illegal for them to use or disclose what they learn. Often embedded in the IC agreement, but worth a standalone document for sensitive work.

Ownership

IP assignment

A separate assignment clause or standalone document that transfers any work product, code, designs, or creative output from the contractor to you. Without this, you have a license to use the work — not ownership of it. For software and custom creative work, this document is non-optional.

Six questions for a contractor engagement.

Who are the parties?

Your business name and the contractor's full legal name or entity name. The agreement uses the correct designations for 1099 classification language.

2

What is the contractor doing?

The specific services or deliverables. BizDraft uses this to populate the scope and to confirm the independent nature of the work relationship.

3

How are they being paid?

Hourly, fixed-price, or milestone-based. Payment schedule and method. Whether there's a kill fee if the engagement ends early.

4

Does IP transfer automatically?

Whether all work product transfers to you on delivery, on payment, or via a separate assignment. BizDraft defaults to transfer-on-payment and flags the risk if you choose otherwise.

5

What information are you sharing?

The type and sensitivity of confidential information the contractor will access. This determines how tightly the NDA is drafted and how long the confidentiality obligation lasts.

6

Are there non-compete restrictions?

Whether the contractor can work for direct competitors while engaged. State law varies significantly on enforceability — BizDraft flags this by state.

What to watch out for

Four contractor red flags that hurt businesses.

No IP assignment clause

The most expensive mistake in contractor agreements. Without a written IP assignment, copyright in creative work defaults to the creator. The contractor legally owns what they built for you.

Employee-like control language

If your contract specifies work hours, requires use of your equipment, or tells the contractor how to do their work (not just what to deliver), the IRS may treat them as an employee — triggering payroll taxes you didn't budget for.

Missing confidentiality obligation

A contractor with access to your client list, pricing, or source code who leaves and takes it with them has no liability without a signed NDA. The obligation must exist before the information is shared.

No termination clause

If the engagement isn't working out, how do you end it? What notice is required, and what happens to in-progress work? Agreements without termination language leave both parties in an unclear position when the relationship sours.

Frequently asked questions

Questions about contractor agreements.

What makes someone a contractor vs. an employee?

The IRS uses a multi-factor test centered on behavioral control, financial control, and relationship type. The key distinction: you control the result, not the method. If you're setting their hours and providing their equipment, you're likely looking at an employment relationship.

Can I include a non-compete clause in a contractor agreement?

In most states, yes — but enforceability varies. California prohibits them almost entirely. States like Texas and Florida enforce them with certain conditions. BizDraft flags this by governing state and defaults to non-solicitation language where non-competes are unenforceable.

Does the IP assignment need to be a separate document?

Not necessarily. BizDraft can embed the IP assignment within the IC agreement. A standalone IP assignment document is useful when you want to record the transfer with the copyright office or when the contractor is signing after the work is already delivered.

What if the contractor refuses to sign an IP assignment?

That's a negotiation point, not necessarily a dealbreaker. Some contractors retain a license while assigning the copyright. Others request a right to show work in their portfolio. These are addressable in the contract — but if they won't assign at all, you may not own what you're paying for.
