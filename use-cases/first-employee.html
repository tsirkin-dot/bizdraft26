What's happening here

The transition from contractor to employee changes everything — legally.

Hiring a W-2 employee is a fundamentally different legal relationship than hiring a contractor. The obligations, protections, and documents required are different — and getting them wrong from the start creates liability that compounds over time.

The situation

You've found the right person. They're coming on full-time — salary or hourly, with benefits. You need to formalize the relationship with the right paperwork: offer letter, employment agreement, and the embedded provisions that protect your business as the relationship begins.

Why it matters

An employment agreement defines the terms of a legally complex relationship governed by federal and state employment law. It clarifies compensation, at-will status, IP ownership, confidentiality, non-solicitation, and what happens at termination. Without it, every one of these areas becomes a potential dispute.

What goes wrong without one

An employee who leaves and takes your customer list. An employee who builds a key product on your time and claims personal IP ownership. Compensation disputes about what was promised and what was delivered. A termination that becomes a wrongful termination claim because the at-will status wasn't clearly established. All preventable at hire.

The right agreement for this situation

Three documents for a new employee.

The employment agreement is the foundation. The offer letter documents the terms at acceptance. The IP and confidentiality provisions — embedded or standalone — protect what the employee will create and learn during their time with you.

Core document

Employment agreement

Defines the employment relationship: role and responsibilities, compensation (salary or hourly), benefits summary, work location, at-will status (in most states), hours expectations, PTO policy, equity if applicable, and termination terms. The governing document for the employment relationship.

IP and confidentiality

PIIA (Proprietary Information and IP Assignment)

A standalone or embedded document assigning all work product created by the employee during employment to the company, establishing confidentiality obligations for business and client information, and defining post-departure obligations. The most important protective document in a first hire.

Offer documentation

Offer letter

A shorter, less formal document confirming the role, start date, compensation, and contingencies (background check, references). Often sent before the employment agreement — the offer letter confirms intent, the employment agreement is the binding contract.

What BizDraft asks you

Six questions for an employment agreement.

1

What is the role and compensation?

Job title, department, salary or hourly rate, overtime eligibility (FLSA exempt vs. non-exempt), pay frequency, and any variable compensation — commission, bonus, or equity. These form the compensation clause.

2

What benefits are included?

Health insurance, dental/vision, 401(k), PTO, sick leave, parental leave, remote work policy. BizDraft includes a benefits summary clause and notes that benefits are subject to the plan documents — not the employment agreement itself.

3

Is employment at-will?

In most US states, employment is at-will — either party can end it at any time for any lawful reason. If there's a fixed term or a specific termination procedure (notice period, cause required), BizDraft adjusts the termination clause accordingly.

4

What IP provisions apply?

Does the company own everything the employee creates during employment? Are there carve-outs for personal projects? BizDraft defaults to full work-product assignment to the company with a carve-out for pre-existing IP and personal projects developed outside work hours.

5

Are there post-employment restrictions?

Non-solicitation of clients and employees, non-compete (where enforceable). BizDraft adjusts based on the state — California prohibits non-competes; Texas and Florida allow them with conditions. Non-solicitation is the default protective provision.

6

What state governs?

Employment law is heavily state-specific — minimum wage, overtime rules, paid leave requirements, non-compete enforceability, final pay timing. BizDraft adjusts the entire agreement based on the state you select.

What to watch out for

Four first-employee mistakes.

Misclassifying as exempt from overtime

FLSA overtime exemptions have specific salary and duties tests. Calling someone "salaried" or "exempt" in a contract doesn't make it so. Misclassifying a non-exempt employee as exempt exposes you to back overtime pay, penalties, and potential class action liability.

No IP assignment at hire

Work-for-hire doctrine covers some employee-created work, but it has gaps — especially for software, creative works, and inventions. An explicit IP assignment in the employment agreement removes ambiguity about who owns what was built on company time.

State-specific paid leave requirements

Many states require paid sick leave, paid family leave, or other mandatory leave — and the minimums change frequently. An employment agreement that doesn't reflect state minimums may be unenforceable in the areas where it conflicts. BizDraft flags known state requirements for your state.

At-will statement buried or absent

In at-will states, an employment agreement that describes performance review procedures, documentation requirements before termination, or "for cause" language without explicitly preserving at-will status may inadvertently create an implied contract limiting your termination rights.

Frequently asked questions

Questions about first employee agreements.

Do I need an employment agreement or just an offer letter?

For a first employee, both. The offer letter confirms the role and compensation at acceptance — before the employment agreement is signed. The employment agreement is the binding contract that covers IP, confidentiality, and all the protections an offer letter doesn't include.

Does an at-will clause mean I can fire someone for any reason?

At-will means you can terminate for any reason except an unlawful one — discrimination based on protected class, retaliation for protected activity, violation of public policy. At-will does not mean unlimited termination authority; it means no contractual requirement for cause. Employment counsel is worth consulting before any termination.

Can I include equity or a bonus in the employment agreement?

Yes. Equity grants (stock options, RSUs) should be referenced in the employment agreement but governed by a separate equity plan document. Bonus structures can be embedded directly. BizDraft includes equity and bonus provisions and flags that equity details are governed by the separate plan documents.

What other paperwork do I need beyond the employment agreement?

Federal requirements: W-4 (tax withholding), I-9 (work authorization verification). State requirements vary — some require new hire reporting within a defined period. You'll also need to register for payroll taxes in your state. The employment agreement is the contractual foundation; HR and tax paperwork are separate compliance requirements.
