What's happening here

The work doesn't end when the project does. The agreement shouldn't either.

A project agreement is built around a finish line. A recurring engagement doesn't have one — and that changes everything about how the contract needs to be structured.

The situation

You're providing (or receiving) ongoing services month over month — accounting, marketing, IT support, legal advisory, content creation, or maintenance. The relationship is ongoing, the fee is recurring, and the scope of what's included needs to be clear.

Why it matters

Without a retainer agreement, every month becomes a renegotiation. What's included in "marketing support"? What happens when you ask for a third blog post in a month that only covers two? Do unused hours roll over or expire? These questions have expensive answers if you haven't defined them in advance.

What goes wrong without one

Scope creep across months, not just projects. Fee disputes when work slows down. Difficulty ending the relationship because there's no notice period. Clients who stop paying and claim the work wasn't what they expected. All preventable with a retainer that defines the terms clearly.

The right agreement for this situation

Three shapes for ongoing work.

The best structure depends on whether the engagement is hour-based, output-based, or availability-based. Each shapes the contract differently.

Standard retainer

Retainer agreement (hours-based)

Fixed monthly fee in exchange for a set number of hours. Defines the included hours, what counts against the retainer, the overage rate, whether hours roll over, and the cancellation notice period. The most common structure for advisory, accounting, and ongoing professional services.

Output-based retainer

Service agreement (recurring deliverables)

Fixed monthly fee for a defined set of deliverables — 4 blog posts, 1 monthly report, 8 social media posts. Scope is defined by output, not hours. Better for content, SEO, design, and other work where hours are hard to predict but deliverables are clear.

Availability-based retainer

Availability retainer

The client is paying for priority access and guaranteed availability — not a specific number of hours or deliverables. Common for legal advisory, executive coaching, and on-call technical support. The fee buys priority access; actual work beyond a threshold is billed additionally.

What BizDraft asks you

Six questions for a retainer agreement.

1

What is the monthly fee?

The fixed monthly amount and when it's due. Is it billed in advance (beginning of month) or in arrears (end of month)? BizDraft drafts the payment terms to match.

2

What does the retainer cover?

Hours, deliverables, or availability? The scope definition is the most important clause in a retainer — it determines what's included, what isn't, and what triggers an overage.

3

Do unused hours roll over?

Some retainers let unused hours carry over to the next month (up to a cap); others expire. This choice affects the financial dynamics of the engagement. BizDraft drafts whichever structure you choose.

4

What is the overage rate?

The hourly or per-unit rate that applies when the client requests work beyond what the retainer covers. This clause prevents uncompensated overages and keeps the billing relationship clean.

5

What is the cancellation notice period?

Typically 30 or 60 days. Either side may want to end the retainer, and the notice period protects the service provider from sudden loss of recurring revenue and the client from abrupt service loss.

6

Is there an initial term?

A minimum commitment period before either side can exit (e.g., 3 months minimum) gives the service provider enough runway to deliver results and the client enough time to evaluate. BizDraft includes an initial term if you specify one.

What to watch out for

Four retainer red flags.

Undefined scope that grows monthly

A retainer that doesn't define what's included invites the client to treat it as unlimited access. Define explicitly what work is covered, what requires a separate scope, and what constitutes an overage — even for trusted long-term clients.

No cancellation clause

A retainer without a defined cancellation process creates a relationship that's hard to end cleanly. Clients can just stop paying. Service providers can just stop showing up. Define the process: notice period, final deliverables, final invoice.

Hourly tracking without a system

Hours-based retainers require tracking to be enforceable. If you're claiming overage charges, you need records. BizDraft's retainer agreement includes a reporting obligation — the service provider provides monthly time summaries.

No annual rate review

A retainer locked at today's rate for three years benefits the client, not the service provider. Include a rate review clause — typically allowing a fee adjustment once annually with 30-day notice — so the retainer remains commercially viable as costs rise.

Frequently asked questions

Questions about retainer agreements.

What's the difference between a retainer and a service agreement?

A service agreement is typically project-based with a defined end. A retainer is ongoing with a recurring fee. The key retainer-specific clauses — rollover, overage rate, cancellation notice — don't exist in a standard service agreement.

Should unused hours roll over or expire?

Expiring hours are simpler to manage and better for service providers (the fee is earned regardless of usage). Rolling hours create goodwill and are preferred by clients, but can create an accruing liability. Capping rollover at one month is a common middle ground.

How long should the minimum commitment period be?

3 months is a common minimum for most retainers. It gives you enough time to demonstrate value without locking the client in for too long. For retainers that require significant upfront work (onboarding, setup, strategy), 6 months is reasonable.

Can I pause a retainer instead of cancelling it?

You can — if your agreement includes a pause clause. BizDraft can include a pause provision that allows either party to suspend the retainer for a defined period (typically 1–2 months, once per contract year) without triggering the full cancellation process.
