What's happening here

You're pitching. You're sharing detailed financial and strategic information. Understand what protection is realistic.

Investor pitches require sharing sensitive information — financials, projections, product roadmaps, customer data, competitive positioning. The practical reality of investor NDAs is different from other business NDAs, and understanding that reality helps you protect yourself more effectively.

The situation

You're in conversations with potential investors — angels, family offices, strategic investors, or smaller VCs. You've been asked for a pitch deck, financial model, customer metrics, or a full data room. You want some form of confidentiality protection before sharing the details.

The reality

Most institutional venture capital firms will not sign an NDA before an initial pitch — it's standard practice in the industry, and they see hundreds of pitches. Angel investors, family offices, and strategic investors are more willing to sign. Knowing who will and won't sign helps you calibrate what to share before any NDA is in place.

What goes wrong without one

An investor who passes on your deal and shares your financial model with a portfolio company that competes with you. A strategic investor who uses your customer data and competitive analysis to inform their own strategy. These aren't hypotheticals. For non-VC conversations, an NDA creates meaningful protection and is worth pursuing.

The right agreement for this situation

Three approaches — calibrated to the type of investor.

The agreement you pursue depends on who you're talking to and how far along the conversation is. There's a meaningful difference between an initial pitch and detailed due diligence.

Angel or family office investors

Mutual NDA (pitch-stage)

A short, clean mutual NDA covering both sides' confidential disclosures during the pitch and due diligence process. Defines the confidential information categories (financials, customer data, product roadmap), establishes a 2–3 year term, and includes standard carve-outs for public information and legally compelled disclosure.

Strategic investors or corporate VCs

Mutual NDA with standstill provision

When a strategic investor (a company in your industry) is evaluating an investment, the risk isn't just that they share your information — it's that they use it competitively. A standstill provision prohibits the investor from making certain competitive moves (soliciting your employees, replicating your product approach) for a defined period after the NDA is signed.

Deep due diligence

Due diligence NDA

Once an investor is serious enough to request a data room — full financials, customer contracts, IP documentation, employee agreements — a more specific NDA governing the data room process is appropriate. This version includes document-specific confidentiality (not just categories), limited personnel access, no-copy restrictions, and return-or-destroy obligations on deal termination.

What BizDraft asks you

Five questions for an investor NDA.

1

Who are the parties?

Your company name and the investor's legal name or entity name. If the investor is an individual, use their full legal name. If it's a fund, use the fund entity name.

2

What information are you protecting?

Financial projections, customer data, product roadmap, proprietary technology, or all of the above. The confidential information definition should be specific enough to be enforceable and broad enough to cover what you're actually sharing.

3

Is the investor a strategic or financial investor?

If the investor operates in your industry or has portfolio companies that compete with you, additional standstill or non-use provisions are worth including. A financial investor (pure capital, no operating presence) typically needs only the standard confidentiality provisions.

4

How long should the NDA last?

2 years is standard for a pitch-stage NDA. For due diligence or data room NDAs, 3 years with a perpetual trade secret carve-out is more appropriate. BizDraft defaults to 2 years and flags if you want to extend for specific information categories.

5

What state governs?

Your state of incorporation. Delaware is common for startup entities; the agreement should specify the state and choice of law for any enforcement action.

What to watch out for

Four investor NDA realities to understand.

Most institutional VCs won't sign

Top-tier VC firms see hundreds of pitches per year across every category. If they signed NDAs for every initial meeting, their entire portfolio would be covered in conflicting confidentiality obligations. Standard practice is no NDA at early stage. If you require an NDA before a first VC meeting, the meeting usually doesn't happen.

An NDA doesn't prevent competition, just secrecy

An NDA prevents disclosure and unauthorized use of specific information. It doesn't prevent the investor from investing in a competitor — unless you've included a standstill provision. Know what the NDA actually covers before assuming it protects your competitive position.

Be careful what you share at initial stage

The better protection in early-stage investor conversations is often information hygiene rather than an NDA. Share high-level financials and the business case in initial conversations. Reserve detailed customer contracts, technical architecture, and proprietary data for later-stage conversations when a formal NDA is appropriate and the investor relationship is established.

Employees are sometimes bound separately

An NDA with an investment firm binds the firm, but the individual partners and associates who saw your deck may not be individually bound unless the firm's NDA specifically extends to its employees. For strategic investors, requiring that named individuals sign ensures coverage even if the firm relationship ends.

Frequently asked questions

Questions about investor NDAs.

Should the NDA be one-way or mutual for an investor pitch?

Mutual NDAs are more likely to be signed because they're perceived as fair — the investor also has confidentiality obligations (though typically lighter ones) on their end. One-way NDAs (you disclose, investor protects) are more common for very early-stage pitches where the investor is sharing nothing sensitive.

Does a pitch deck NDA cover verbal information shared in the meeting?

A well-drafted NDA covers information shared in writing, verbally, or by demonstration. Some NDAs require verbal disclosures to be confirmed in writing within a defined period to be covered. BizDraft's default covers both written and oral disclosures made in connection with the defined purpose.

What's a standstill provision and do I need one?

A standstill prohibits the investor from taking specific actions — buying shares in competitors, hiring your employees, replicating your approach — for a defined period. It's most relevant when a strategic investor (operating company) is the other party. For financial investors, a standard NDA without a standstill is usually sufficient.

When is it appropriate to ask for an NDA?

In the investor context: before sharing detailed financial models, customer-level data, proprietary technology documentation, or anything that's genuinely competitively sensitive. Not for an initial pitch deck with general market and team information — that information is typically not sensitive enough to require NDA protection and asking for one at that stage can signal naivety to sophisticated investors.

Does an NDA with an investor affect future fundraising rounds?

It can if the NDA is poorly drafted. An overly broad NDA could theoretically conflict with your ability to share information in future fundraising. Most investor NDAs include a carve-out for information that becomes public through no fault of either party and for disclosures to advisors and counsel. Make sure your NDA includes these standard exceptions.
