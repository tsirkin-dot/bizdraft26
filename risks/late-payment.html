Late payment.
The invoice that ages.

Work is done. Invoice is sent. Days pass. Then weeks. The client is "getting to it." Your cash flow isn't.

Draft an agreement that prevents it

What it means

In plain English.

Late payment is what happens when a client takes longer to pay than you agreed — or, more often, than you didn't agree, because the contract never specified a due date, a consequence, or a remedy.

What it looks like

"Invoice received — processing." "Finance team is backed up this week." "Check is in the mail." Thirty days becomes sixty. The work was delivered on time. The payment wasn't.

Why it's so common

Most agreements don't specify a hard due date, a penalty for lateness, or the right to stop work. Without teeth, the invoice is just a suggestion. Clients with their own cash flow pressure optimize accordingly.

Why it's expensive

You paid your suppliers, your staff, your software — on time. Your client didn't extend you the same courtesy. The gap between outflows and inflows is what kills small businesses, not bad clients.

Why it's missed

It feels awkward to write consequences into a new relationship.

Why missed

Starting on good faith

You're excited to work with them. You don't want to signal distrust before the project starts. The late-fee clause gets left out in the spirit of goodwill.

Why missed

No due date written in

Many contracts say "payment due upon completion" or "net 30" without defining when day one starts. "Net 30 from when?" becomes a dispute.

Why missed

No escalation path

Without a late fee or suspension-of-work clause, you have no lever. You can send a polite email. That's it. The client knows it.

What can go wrong

The downstream consequences.

Cash flow crunch

Your commitments don't pause because the client is late. Payroll, rent, software — all due on schedule. The receivable gap becomes your problem to bridge.

Ongoing work continues unpaid

Without a suspension clause, you may be obligated to keep delivering even while the invoice sits unpaid. More exposure, same problem.

Debt becomes bad debt

The longer an invoice ages, the lower the collection probability. At 90 days, most small business receivables are effectively uncollectable without legal action.

Collection cost exceeds the invoice

Small claims or a collections agency costs money and time. The $2,000 invoice becomes a $1,200 recovery after fees. The contract was the cheaper fix.

Red-flag wording

If your contract says any of this, late payment already has a door.

These phrases are everywhere in boilerplate. They feel professional. They provide no protection.

Red flag

"upon completion"

When is completion? Who decides? If the client disputes whether work is done, this phrase starts the payment clock nowhere.

Red flag

"net 30" with no trigger

Net 30 from invoice date? Delivery date? Approval? "Net 30" alone is a range, not a date. Disputed start = disputed due date.

Red flag

"payment as agreed"

Agreed where? In an email? A phone call? Not in the contract, where it can be enforced.

Red flag

No late fee clause

Without a stated consequence for lateness, there is no consequence. You can ask. You can't enforce.

Red flag

No suspension right

If you can't pause work for non-payment, you're obligated to keep delivering into a non-paying relationship. That clause matters.

Safer pattern

The four clauses that close the gap.

Late payment protection isn't one clause — it's a system. A deposit clause gets money before work starts. A milestone clause ties delivery to payment checkpoints. A late-fee clause creates a cost for delay. A suspension clause gives you the right to stop.

BizDraft includes all four by default, calibrated to the project type and payment schedule you describe.

The pattern

  • Deposit clause. "A deposit of [X]% is due before work begins. Work does not commence until the deposit is received."
  • Milestone payments. "Payment is due at the following milestones: [list]. Delivery of the subsequent milestone is contingent on receipt of the prior milestone payment."
  • Late fee. "Invoices not paid within [N] days of the invoice date are subject to a late fee of [X]% per month on the outstanding balance."
  • Suspension right. "Provider reserves the right to suspend all work if any payment is more than [N] days overdue. Work resumes upon receipt of overdue amounts plus any applicable late fees."
