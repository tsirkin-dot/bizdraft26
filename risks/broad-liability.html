One mistake, unlimited exposure.

What it means

In plain English.

Broad liability is when a contract — through open-ended indemnity clauses, no liability cap, or uncapped consequential damages — exposes you to financial responsibility far exceeding the value of the engagement. Without limitation-of-liability language, a single professional mistake can lead to an unlimited financial claim.

What it looks like

A developer delivers a website with a security flaw. The client suffers a data breach. Their losses: $300,000. Your contract says you "shall indemnify Client for any damages arising from the services." No cap. No carve-out.

The two exposure types

Direct damages (the cost of fixing the mistake) and consequential damages (the downstream business losses caused by the mistake). Consequential damages — lost revenue, customer churn, reputational harm — are where uncapped liability becomes catastrophic.

Why it's so common

Many contracts don't include liability language at all. Others include indemnity clauses drafted for large-company risk profiles — applied to small engagements where the same language creates asymmetric exposure.

Why it's missed

The worst case feels too unlikely to plan for. Until it isn't.

Why missed

The engagement seems low-risk

A logo project doesn't feel like a liability risk. But what if the logo infringes on a registered trademark? Downstream brand costs are consequential damages — potentially unlimited without a cap.

Why missed

Indemnity clause accepted without reading

Many providers sign client-supplied contracts with broad indemnity clauses they didn't write and didn't negotiate. The exposure is buried in standard legal language.

Why missed

No professional liability insurance

Many small businesses assume they'll never face a liability claim. They don't have E&O insurance and don't have a cap in the contract. Both absences create the same exposure.

What can go wrong

The downstream consequences.

Claim exceeds your business value

A $10,000 engagement with unlimited liability exposure can generate a seven-figure claim. Without a cap, the entire claim falls to you.

Consequential damages snowball

The client's lost revenue, customer churn, and reputational harm can dwarf the cost of the original mistake. These are consequential damages — and without a carve-out, they're yours to absorb.

Third-party indemnity

Broad indemnity language can require you to defend the client against third-party claims — even claims that have nothing to do with your specific work.

Personal liability risk

For sole proprietors and single-member LLCs without proper structure, broad liability clauses can pierce through to personal assets if your entity protections aren't solid.

Red-flag wording

These phrases are open-ended liability traps.

They appear in client-supplied contracts, standard templates, and boilerplate MSAs. They create asymmetric exposure that small providers can't survive.

Red flag

"shall indemnify for any and all damages"

"Any and all" means direct, indirect, consequential, incidental, and special damages — often in amounts you cannot predict or cover.

Red flag

"including lost profits and revenue"

Consequential damages explicitly included. The client's revenue losses become your liability. This needs a carve-out.

Red flag

No limitation of liability section

The most common version. The contract simply says nothing about limits. Without a cap, courts can award the full amount of proven damages.

Red flag

"Provider is fully responsible for"

"Fully responsible" without a cap is unlimited responsibility. Add a cap and a consequential damages exclusion immediately after.

Safer pattern

The liability cap and consequential damages carve-out.

Two clauses work together: a liability cap that puts an upper bound on your total financial exposure, and a consequential damages exclusion that removes downstream business losses from the scope of your liability. Both are standard in professional services contracts at every level.

BizDraft includes both by default, with the cap calibrated to the engagement value you specify.

The pattern

  • Liability cap. "Provider's total aggregate liability to Client under this Agreement shall not exceed the total fees paid by Client to Provider in the [three months / twelve months] preceding the claim."
  • Consequential damages exclusion. "In no event shall either party be liable for any indirect, incidental, special, consequential, or punitive damages, including but not limited to lost profits, lost revenue, or loss of goodwill, even if advised of the possibility of such damages."
  • Carve-outs for intentional misconduct. "The limitations above do not apply to damages arising from gross negligence, willful misconduct, or fraud."
  • Mutual application. "The limitations in this section apply equally to both parties." — This makes the clause less likely to be struck as unconscionable.
