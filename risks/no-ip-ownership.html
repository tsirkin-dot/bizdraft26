No IP ownership trigger.
Work without a transfer.

The client thinks they own it. The creator still does. Default copyright law didn't care about the invoice — it cares about who made it.

What it means

In plain English.

Under U.S. copyright law, the person who creates a work owns it — unless there's a written assignment to the contrary. Paying for work doesn't transfer ownership. A verbal agreement doesn't transfer ownership. Only a written assignment does. If the contract doesn't include one, the creator retains the copyright.

What it looks like

A client pays a designer for a logo, uses it for three years, then discovers the designer still owns the copyright. The contract said "client owns the work" but never included a formal assignment — which means it probably didn't transfer under copyright law.

The default rule

Copyright vests in the author at creation. The only exceptions are employees (work-for-hire) and certain commissioned works with a written agreement. Independent contractors don't qualify for work-for-hire by default.

Why it matters to both sides

Clients want certainty that they own what they paid for. Creators need to decide whether to assign, license, or retain rights — and on what terms. Silence serves neither party well.

Why it's missed

Everyone assumes payment = ownership. Copyright law disagrees.

Why missed

The invoice says "full rights"

An invoice is not a contract. Even a contract that says "full rights" without a formal assignment clause may not effect a valid copyright transfer under 17 U.S.C. § 204.

Why missed

No trigger event defined

Even when an assignment clause exists, it often doesn't state when ownership transfers. On signing? On payment? On delivery? The trigger matters.

Why missed

Creators don't realize the leverage

Retaining copyright until payment is received is one of the most powerful protections available to freelancers and independent contractors — and most never use it.

What can go wrong

The downstream consequences.

Client can't legally use the work

If no valid assignment occurred, the client is using work they don't own. Technically, the creator can pursue an infringement claim — even years later.

Creator can't protect their portfolio

When ownership is unclear, so is what you can show, license, or sell. Portfolio rights, derivative works, resale — all need a clear baseline.

Payment leverage disappears

Retaining copyright until final payment is a powerful collection tool. No IP clause = no leverage over non-paying clients.

Dispute at exit

When the relationship ends or the client wants to sell the business, ambiguous IP ownership surfaces as a deal-blocking problem.

Red-flag wording

These phrases don't actually transfer IP.

They feel like ownership language. They often aren't, without the formal assignment structure that copyright law requires.

Red flag

"Client owns all work product"

Without an "hereby assigns" clause and both parties' signatures, this statement may not constitute a valid written transfer under copyright law.

Red flag

"Full rights included"

What rights? License or assignment? Exclusive or non-exclusive? "Full rights" is meaningless without specificity.

Red flag

No IP clause at all

The most common situation. The contract is silent on ownership. The default rule applies: creator owns it.

Red flag

"Work for hire"

For independent contractors, work-for-hire only applies to specific categories of works listed in the copyright statute. Calling it "work for hire" doesn't make it one.

Safer pattern

The clause that makes the transfer real.

A valid IP assignment requires: (1) a written instrument, (2) signed by the transferor, (3) identifying the work, (4) stating the transfer. The trigger event — when ownership moves — should also be specified, typically tied to full payment.

BizDraft includes an IP assignment clause by default for creative and development agreements, with payment-contingent transfer language.

The pattern

  • Assignment language. "Provider hereby assigns to Client all right, title, and interest in the Work, including all copyright, upon receipt of full payment."
  • Payment-contingent trigger. "Assignment is effective upon Provider's receipt of all amounts due under this Agreement. Prior to full payment, Provider grants Client a limited license to use the Work for the purpose described in this Agreement only."
  • Creator's retained rights. "Provider retains the right to display the Work in a professional portfolio and to use the Work as a sample of services, unless Client requests otherwise in writing."
  • Pre-existing materials carved out. "The foregoing assignment does not include Provider's pre-existing materials, tools, or processes incorporated in the Work. Provider grants Client a non-exclusive license to use such materials as incorporated."
