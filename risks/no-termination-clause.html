No termination clause.
No clean exit.

The project goes sideways. Both parties want out. Without a termination clause, neither knows how — or what the other is owed on the way out the door.

What it means

In plain English.

A termination clause defines the conditions under which either party can end the agreement, what notice is required, and what happens to work and money when they do. Without one, ending a contract is legally murky — which means it's expensive, contentious, or both.

What it looks like

The client wants to cancel with the project 60% done. There's no termination clause. Do they owe for work completed? Is the deposit refundable? Do you have to hand over unfinished files? Nobody knows.

The two termination types

Termination for cause (material breach, non-payment) and termination for convenience (either party, no reason needed). Both need notice requirements and financial consequences spelled out separately.

Why it matters to both parties

Clients need to know they're not locked in forever if things go wrong. Providers need to know they'll be compensated for work already done. A termination clause serves both needs.

Why it's missed

Planning the end of a project at the beginning feels pessimistic. It isn't.

Why missed

Optimism bias

Neither party enters a contract expecting it to fail. The termination clause feels like planning for failure. It's actually planning for reality — some contracts don't run to completion.

Why missed

Short projects feel low-risk

For a two-week engagement, a termination clause seems excessive. But short engagements still have deposits, work in progress, and unclear obligations at cancellation.

Why missed

Assumed mutual understanding

"If either of us wants to stop, we just stop." Until you stop and the other party disputes what's owed. The mutual understanding disappears in a dispute.

What can go wrong

The downstream consequences.

Trapped in a bad engagement

Without a termination clause, you may have to keep working with a client who's not paying, not communicating, or actively making the project worse.

No compensation for work done

Client walks. You've done 40 hours of work. Without a termination clause specifying payment for work completed, you may be owed nothing if the project wasn't "finished."

File delivery dispute

When the relationship ends mid-project, what do you hand over? What do you keep? Without a clause, this becomes a negotiation under pressure.

Post-termination obligations unclear

Confidentiality, IP ownership, non-compete provisions — do they survive termination? Silence often means they don't. Specify what continues.

Red-flag wording

Signs your contract has no safe exit.

These are the absences and vague phrases that signal there's no clean way out.

Red flag

No termination section

The most common version of this risk. The contract simply doesn't address ending the relationship. Default rules are unclear and costly to resolve.

Red flag

"Either party may terminate at will"

Without specifying what's owed at termination, "at will" is an invitation to walk out without paying for work already done.

Red flag

No notice period

How much notice is required before termination takes effect? Without a number, the answer is "immediately." That's rarely what either party actually wants.

Red flag

No work-in-progress provision

What happens to half-done work? Who owns it? Who pays for it? The contract should answer before the situation arises.

Safer pattern

The three-part termination clause.

A clean termination clause has three parts: notice, financial settlement, and file handling. Each is short. Each prevents a different dispute. Together they make ending a contract as straightforward as starting one.

BizDraft includes termination provisions by default, covering both for-cause and for-convenience scenarios with notice periods and compensation provisions calibrated to the engagement.

The pattern

  • Notice requirement. "Either party may terminate this Agreement with [N] days' written notice. Notice is effective on the date sent to the other party's designated email address."
  • Compensation for work completed. "Upon termination, Client shall pay for all work completed through the notice period at the pro-rated contract rate. Work-in-progress becomes payable at the agreed hourly rate."
  • Kill fee for convenience termination. "Termination by Client for convenience (without cause) is subject to a kill fee of [X]% of the remaining contract value."
  • File delivery. "Upon full payment of amounts owed, Provider will deliver all completed work product. Work product not paid for at the time of termination remains Provider's property until payment is received."
  • Survival clause. "Sections on confidentiality, IP ownership, limitation of liability, and dispute resolution survive termination of this Agreement."
