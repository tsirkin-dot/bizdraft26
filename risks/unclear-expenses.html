Unclear expenses.
Who pays for the hotel?

The project requires travel. Or a software license. Or stock photos. Or printing. Nobody said who was paying. The invoice arrives. The dispute begins.

What it means

In plain English.

Unclear expenses is when the contract doesn't address out-of-pocket costs incurred by the provider on behalf of the project. Travel, accommodation, software, stock imagery, printing, materials, permits — all of these can be significant, and all of them can become disputes when the contract is silent.

What it looks like

The consultant travels to the client's office for two days. Flights, hotel, and meals total $1,200. The contract says nothing about expenses. The client expected them to be included. The consultant expected to be reimbursed. Neither said so.

The common categories

Travel and accommodation. Software licenses. Stock photography or video. Materials and supplies. Printing and production. Permits and filing fees. Third-party services procured on client's behalf. Each needs explicit treatment in the contract.

Why pre-approval matters

The cleanest expense clause requires pre-approval for any expense above a threshold. This eliminates retroactive disputes entirely: if the client approved it, they pay for it. If they didn't approve it, the provider absorbs it.

Why it's missed

Expenses seem like common sense. They aren't always.

Why missed

Expenses seem incidental

At contract time, expenses don't seem significant enough to discuss. By project end, a travel-heavy engagement can have incurred thousands in unreimbursed costs.

Why missed

The rate assumed to cover everything

Clients sometimes assume the quoted rate includes all expenses. Providers sometimes quote a rate expecting to bill expenses separately. Neither assumption is written down.

Why missed

No pre-approval process

Even when expenses are acknowledged, without a pre-approval process, every expense bill is a potential dispute. Pre-approval converts a retroactive argument into a proactive agreement.

What can go wrong

The downstream consequences.

Unrecoverable out-of-pocket costs

The provider incurs real costs — flights, hotels, software — that the client disputes. Without a contract clause, the provider has no legal basis for reimbursement.

Invoice dispute at project end

The final invoice includes expenses. The client disputes items they say were never authorized. What should be a simple closing becomes a negotiation.

Margin erosion

When expenses are absorbed into the project fee without explicit treatment, the effective margin on the engagement is lower than quoted. On travel-heavy projects, expenses can represent 20-30% of total cost.

Scope of "included" never defined

The client believes expenses are included in the rate. The provider believes they're additional. Both assumptions are reasonable absent a contract clause. Write it down.

Red-flag wording

These phrases leave expenses undefined.

The most common gap is silence. These phrases also create ambiguity about who pays what.

Red flag

No expense clause

Nothing in the contract about expenses. Both parties bring their own assumptions. Disputes are guaranteed when significant expenses arise.

Red flag

"Expenses as incurred"

As incurred by whom? What category of expenses? Without a definition, any expense the provider incurs is arguably "as incurred."

Red flag

"Reasonable expenses reimbursed"

Reasonable by whose standard? Without a pre-approval process or category list, "reasonable" is disputed after the fact.

Red flag

No threshold for pre-approval

Even when expenses are acknowledged, no pre-approval threshold means every expense is a retroactive negotiation.

Safer pattern

The pre-approval expense clause that ends the dispute before it starts.

A clean expense clause does three things: defines what categories of expenses are reimbursable, sets a threshold above which pre-approval is required, and specifies how expenses are documented and billed. Each element prevents a different kind of dispute.

BizDraft includes an expense clause by default in consulting and travel-involving service agreements, with a pre-approval threshold and documentation requirement.

The pattern

  • Define reimbursable categories. "Client shall reimburse Provider for pre-approved out-of-pocket expenses reasonably incurred in connection with the Services, including: travel (economy class), accommodation, meals (up to $[X]/day), software licenses required for the project, and materials."
  • Pre-approval threshold. "Expenses below $[threshold] per item do not require prior approval. Expenses of $[threshold] or more require written approval from Client before being incurred."
  • Documentation requirement. "Provider shall submit receipts for all reimbursable expenses. Expenses without receipts will not be reimbursed."
  • Billing schedule. "Expenses are billed monthly with Provider's invoice. Payment is due within [N] days of the invoice date."
