No dispute resolution.
Default = court.

There's a disagreement. Nothing in the contract says how to resolve it. The default answer: file a lawsuit. That's the most expensive version of every business dispute.

What it means

In plain English.

A dispute resolution clause specifies how disagreements will be handled before either party resorts to litigation. Without one, both parties default to the court system — which is slow, public, expensive, and often disproportionate to the value of the original contract.

The default without a clause

No clause means disputes go to court in whatever jurisdiction is applicable — often unclear for contracts between parties in different states. Filing fees, attorney fees, and months of process for disputes worth a few thousand dollars.

Arbitration vs. litigation

Arbitration is a private, faster, less formal process. For most small business disputes, it's cheaper and faster than court. Many service contracts include mandatory arbitration for exactly this reason.

Governing law implications

Without a governing law clause, disputes trigger a conflict-of-law analysis to determine which state's law applies. That analysis can add cost and uncertainty before the actual dispute is even addressed.

Why it's missed

Nobody plans to have a dispute. Contracts should plan for them anyway.

Why missed

Feels unnecessarily adversarial

Including dispute resolution language feels like you're expecting the relationship to fail. In practice, it's infrastructure that almost never gets used — but is essential when it does.

Why missed

Small contract, small risk assumption

A $3,000 project doesn't seem to warrant dispute resolution language. But a $3,000 dispute in court costs more than $3,000 to resolve. The clause prevents the disproportionate cost.

Why missed

Governing law overlooked

Governing law is sometimes omitted even when dispute resolution is included. These need to work together — the resolution clause should specify which state's law governs.

What can go wrong

The downstream consequences.

Court costs exceed the dispute value

Filing fees, attorney fees, and time — for a $5,000 dispute. The resolution process costs more than the amount in dispute.

Jurisdiction is unclear

The client is in New York. You're in Texas. No governing law clause. Both parties are in different state courts arguing about which court has jurisdiction before the actual dispute is heard.

Public record

Court cases are public. Arbitration is private. A public dispute can damage both parties' business reputations regardless of who wins.

No escalation ladder

Without a clause requiring informal negotiation and then mediation before arbitration or litigation, disputes escalate immediately to the most expensive mechanism.

Red-flag wording

Signs your contract has no dispute path.

The most common situation is simply no dispute clause at all. These phrases also signal unresolved gaps.

Red flag

No dispute resolution section

Default to court. No escalation path. Most expensive outcome for both parties.

Red flag

No governing law clause

Without a governing law clause, the applicable law is determined by a court, not the contract. Add uncertainty to an already disputed situation.

Red flag

"Disputes will be resolved by mutual agreement"

What if there's no mutual agreement? This phrase describes the problem, not the solution.

Red flag

No attorney fee provision

Without a prevailing party attorney fee clause, each side pays their own legal costs. That often makes resolution economically irrational for the party in the right.

Safer pattern

The escalation ladder that keeps disputes out of court.

Best practice is an escalation ladder: informal negotiation first, then mediation, then binding arbitration. Each step is faster and cheaper than the next. Most disputes resolve at step one or two. Arbitration is the safety net.

BizDraft includes a three-step dispute resolution clause by default, with governing law and arbitration venue calibrated to your location.

The pattern

  • Governing law. "This Agreement is governed by and construed in accordance with the laws of the State of [State], without regard to conflict of law principles."
  • Step 1 — informal negotiation. "Parties shall first attempt to resolve disputes through good-faith informal negotiation for [30] days before escalating to formal proceedings."
  • Step 2 — mediation. "If informal negotiation fails, parties agree to non-binding mediation through [JAMS / AAA] before proceeding to arbitration."
  • Step 3 — binding arbitration. "Any dispute not resolved by negotiation or mediation shall be resolved by binding arbitration under the rules of [AAA / JAMS], conducted in [City, State]."
  • Fee provision. "The prevailing party in any arbitration shall be entitled to recover reasonable attorney fees and costs."
