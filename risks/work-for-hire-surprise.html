Work-for-hire surprise.
You thought you owned it.

The client called it "work for hire." The copyright statute has a nine-category list. Your project probably wasn't on it. The creator still owns it — and nobody told you.

What it means

In plain English.

Under U.S. copyright law (17 U.S.C. § 101), a "work made for hire" is one where the employer or commissioning party automatically owns the copyright. For employees, it's automatic. For independent contractors, it only applies to nine specific categories of works — and only if there's a written agreement calling it work-for-hire. Everything else stays with the creator.

The nine categories

Contributions to collective works, parts of motion pictures, translations, supplementary works, compilations, instructional texts, tests, answer material for tests, and atlases. If your project isn't one of these, "work for hire" in the contract doesn't transfer copyright.

What most contracts get wrong

Clients write "this is a work for hire" into contracts for logos, websites, and marketing copy — none of which are on the nine-category list. The language doesn't accomplish a copyright transfer. A written assignment does.

The creator's perspective

Creators who sign "work for hire" agreements for qualifying works give up more than copyright — they give up termination rights (the right to reclaim the copyright after 35 years). This is a significant right being unknowingly waived.

Why it's missed

"Work for hire" sounds like a complete sentence. It isn't.

Why missed

The phrase sounds definitive

"This is a work-for-hire engagement" sounds like it resolves ownership. It only does for works in the nine categories. For everything else, it's just words.

Why missed

Both parties assume it works

The client assumes they're getting ownership. The creator assumes they're giving it. Neither checks whether the work type qualifies. The assumption holds until a dispute surfaces it.

Why missed

Assignment is the better tool

A written copyright assignment accomplishes what work-for-hire language tries to — without the nine-category constraint. Most creators and clients don't know this, so they use the wrong tool.

What can go wrong

The downstream consequences.

Client doesn't own what they paid for

Years after the engagement, it surfaces that the work-for-hire clause didn't transfer copyright. The creator still owns the logo the client has been using for three years.

Creator has unintended leverage

When the relationship sours, the creator may realize they still hold copyright. An unintended ownership dispute becomes a tool in a payment or other dispute.

Business sale complications

When the client tries to sell their business, due diligence reveals they don't legally own the core creative assets. Deal delays or fails.

Creator waives termination rights unknowingly

If the work does qualify for work-for-hire, the creator loses termination rights under Section 203. For a creator who didn't understand this, it's a significant long-term cost.

Red-flag wording

These phrases don't reliably transfer ownership.

Common in client-supplied contracts and online templates. All of them create ownership uncertainty.

Red flag

"This is a work made for hire"

If the work isn't in one of the nine qualifying categories, this sentence doesn't transfer copyright to the commissioning party — regardless of what both parties intended.

Red flag

"Work-for-hire engagement"

Same problem, different phrasing. Calling the engagement "work for hire" doesn't make the work product qualify as work for hire under the Copyright Act.

Red flag

No assignment backup

Even contracts with work-for-hire language should include a fallback assignment clause: "To the extent the foregoing does not constitute a work made for hire, Creator hereby assigns all right, title, and interest to Client."

Red flag

"All intellectual property belongs to client"

Without a formal written assignment signed by the creator, this statement may not constitute a valid copyright transfer under 17 U.S.C. § 204.

Safer pattern

The belt-and-suspenders IP clause.

The safest approach is a two-layer clause: work-for-hire language for qualifying works, plus a written assignment for everything else. This combination covers all scenarios and doesn't rely on the work qualifying for any particular category.

BizDraft uses this belt-and-suspenders approach by default in all creative and development agreements.

The pattern

  • Work-for-hire first layer. "To the extent any Work created under this Agreement qualifies as a 'work made for hire' under 17 U.S.C. § 101, such Work is made for hire and Client shall be deemed the author."
  • Assignment fallback. "To the extent any Work does not qualify as a work made for hire, Creator hereby irrevocably assigns to Client all right, title, and interest in and to such Work, including all copyright."
  • Payment condition. "The foregoing assignment is effective upon Client's receipt of full payment of all amounts due under this Agreement."
  • Creator's retained rights. "Creator retains the right to display Work in a professional portfolio unless otherwise agreed in writing."
