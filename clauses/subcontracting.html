Subcontracting.
The clause that governs who's actually doing the work.

The client hired you. You hired someone else to do part of it. Who's responsible when something goes wrong? Who owns what they created? Without a subcontracting clause, these questions have messy answers.

What it means

In plain English.

A subcontracting clause specifies whether the provider is permitted to engage subcontractors, whether client approval is required, what obligations flow down to subcontractors (particularly confidentiality and IP), and who is responsible for subcontractor performance and deliverables.

Plain English definition

The subcontracting clause answers: can you bring in outside help? Does the client get to approve who? If a subcontractor causes a problem or creates something, who's responsible to the client, who owns the work, and does the subcontractor have to sign confidentiality terms?

When you need it

Any provider who might use subcontractors, freelancers, or outside specialists. This includes agencies (who always use subcontractors), solo providers who bring in occasional help, and any engagement where specialized skills may be needed that aren't in the provider's core team.

What happens without it

Without a subcontracting clause, the client may have a claim that you breached the contract by using someone they didn't approve. IP created by subcontractors may not be properly assigned up the chain. Confidential client information shared with subcontractors may not be protected by any contractual obligation.

Why it's missed

Providers assume they can use subcontractors freely. Clients assume otherwise.

Why missed

The provider assumes it's their business

Most providers assume that how they staff their work is their business — the client hired the result, not the specific person. But many clients have strong opinions about who accesses their information and does their work.

Why missed

The IP chain problem isn't visible

When a subcontractor creates work, they own it by default (unless their contract says otherwise). If the provider doesn't have a proper work-for-hire or assignment clause with the subcontractor, the provider can't transfer ownership to the client. The IP chain breaks silently.

Why missed

Confidentiality flows aren't considered

Provider signs a confidentiality agreement with client. Provider engages a subcontractor who receives the same confidential information. The subcontractor signed nothing with the client. The confidentiality chain is broken unless the provider's subcontract includes matching obligations.

What can go wrong

The downstream consequences.

Broken IP chain

Subcontractor creates a key piece of the deliverable. Provider's contract with client says provider assigns all IP. But the subcontractor never assigned IP to the provider. The chain breaks. Client may not actually own what they paid for.

Confidentiality leak

Subcontractor receives sensitive client information. They're not bound by any confidentiality obligation to the client, and only informally to the provider. Information leaks. Client has no direct claim against the subcontractor, and claims the provider is responsible for the breach.

Unapproved subcontractors

Client has a policy against using offshore resources, or competitors, or specific individuals. Provider uses them anyway because there's no clause requiring approval. Client terminates for cause when they discover it — potentially justifiably.

Subcontractor quality liability

Subcontractor delivers poor work. Provider delivers it to client. Client claims breach. Provider tries to pass liability to the subcontractor, but the client has no direct relationship with them. The provider is fully liable regardless of the subcontractor's fault.

Red-flag wording

Subcontracting language that creates gaps.

These formulations are common. Each one leaves a liability, IP, or confidentiality gap that materializes when something goes wrong.

Red flag

"No subcontracting without client consent"

Consent not defined. Does that mean prior written consent for each subcontractor? Or informal approval? The clause creates a requirement but no mechanism, meaning any use of subcontractors becomes a potential breach.

Red flag

"Subcontractors subject to similar terms"

"Similar" is not "same." Flow-down obligations need to specifically name what's required: confidentiality, IP assignment, and compliance with the prime agreement's key terms.

Red flag

"Provider not responsible for subcontractor errors"

Not enforceable from the client's perspective. The provider is the party in privity of contract with the client. Subcontractor errors are provider errors in the eyes of the client agreement. This clause is void on its face.

Red flag

No subcontracting clause at all

Silence leaves the permission, the IP chain, the confidentiality flow-down, and the approval mechanism all undefined. Both sides assume different things.

Safer pattern

A subcontracting clause that closes the gaps.

A well-drafted subcontracting clause grants permission to subcontract (usually with notice or approval), imposes flow-down obligations on confidentiality and IP, maintains prime contractor responsibility for subcontractor performance, and ensures the IP chain is unbroken from subcontractor to client.

The pattern

  • Grant conditional permission. "Contractor may engage subcontractors to perform portions of the work with prior written notice to Client. Client may object to a specific subcontractor within 5 business days of notice; objection must be based on a reasonable business concern."
  • Require flow-down of key obligations. "All subcontractors must be bound by confidentiality and IP assignment obligations at least as protective as those in this agreement. Contractor is responsible for ensuring compliance."
  • Maintain primary liability. "Contractor remains fully responsible for the performance, quality, and deliverables of any subcontractor engaged under this agreement. Client has no direct relationship or recourse with subcontractors."
  • Ensure the IP chain. "Contractor shall obtain from each subcontractor a written assignment of all IP in work created for this engagement before delivering any such work to Client."
  • Address non-solicitation extension. "Client's non-solicitation obligations under Section [X] extend to subcontractors with whom Client had material contact during the engagement."
