Indemnification.
The clause that decides who pays when a stranger sues.

A third party files a copyright claim against your client, based on content you delivered. Who pays to defend it? Without an indemnification clause, the answer depends on a judge — not your contract.

What it means

In plain English.

Indemnification is a contractual obligation to cover another party's costs when a third party makes a claim against them. It's distinct from the limitation of liability clause — which governs claims between the two contracting parties. Indemnification governs what happens when someone outside the contract brings a claim because of something one of the parties did.

Plain English definition

Party A indemnifies Party B means: if Party C sues Party B because of something Party A did, Party A will cover Party B's defense costs, settlements, and judgments. The classic scenario: a client is sued for copyright infringement because a designer used unlicensed images in the deliverable.

When you need it

Any engagement where your deliverables could expose the client to third-party claims (IP infringement, defamation, privacy violations), or where the client provides you materials that could expose you to claims. Creative services, marketing, content production, and software development all carry third-party claim risk.

What happens without it

Without an indemnification clause, when a third party sues your client over your deliverable, they have two options: absorb the cost themselves, or sue you separately for contribution. Either way, there's no clear contractual framework — which means expensive litigation to establish who owes what.

Why it's missed

Third-party claims feel like an abstract possibility until they happen.

Why missed

The clause feels lawyerly

Indemnification language is dense and unfamiliar to most small business owners. The reaction is to skip it rather than understand it. But skipping it means the allocation of third-party risk is undefined — and undefined means expensive.

Why missed

Liability cap confusion

Many owners assume the limitation of liability clause covers everything, including third-party claims. It typically doesn't. The LOL clause covers claims between the parties. Indemnification covers third-party claims — a separate category entirely.

Why missed

One-sided indemnification accepted by default

Clients often include one-sided indemnification in their standard contracts — provider indemnifies client for everything. Many providers accept without reading. A mutual indemnification is standard; one-sided is a significant risk to negotiate out.

What can go wrong

The downstream consequences.

IP infringement passed down

Designer uses stock imagery without proper licensing. Client publishes the work. Copyright holder sues the client. Client passes the defense cost and damages to the designer under the indemnification clause — or sues the designer if there isn't one.

Content liability

Writer creates content that is allegedly defamatory. Client is named in a defamation suit. Without indemnification, both parties are separately on the hook. With it, responsibility is clearly allocated to whoever caused the problem.

Client materials creating provider liability

Client gives contractor their customer data to process. Data breach occurs. Third parties sue the contractor. Without a clause indemnifying the contractor for liabilities arising from client-provided materials, the contractor bears the full cost.

Defense costs before resolution

Even winning the lawsuit costs money. Defense costs — attorney's fees, expert witnesses, court costs — mount before any judgment is entered. Without indemnification, whoever gets named in the suit bears that cost regardless of fault.

Red-flag wording

Indemnification language that creates problems.

These formulations are common in client-drafted contracts and poorly drafted service agreements. Each one creates unacceptable exposure.

Red flag

"Contractor indemnifies Client for all claims"

One-sided unlimited indemnification. You indemnify the client for any claim of any kind — including claims arising from the client's own materials or instructions. Never accept this without substantial revision.

Red flag

"Including claims arising from contractor's negligence"

This makes indemnification cover your own errors, even when you're the defendant. Indemnification should cover claims arising from your conduct — but separate from your direct liability to the client for your own mistakes.

Red flag

No mutual indemnification

A clause that only runs one way — provider to client — gives the client no incentive to be careful about what materials or instructions they provide. Mutual indemnification aligns incentives for both parties.

Red flag

"Including attorneys' fees and all costs"

Standard indemnification includes attorneys' fees. But "all costs" can mean anything. Define what's covered: reasonable attorneys' fees, court costs, and settlements approved in advance — not open-ended cost absorption.

Safer pattern

An indemnification clause that allocates risk fairly.

A fair indemnification clause is mutual, cause-based, and clearly scoped. Each party indemnifies the other for third-party claims arising from that party's own acts or omissions — not blanket indemnification for everything, and not one-sided.

The pattern

  • Make it mutual. "Each party agrees to indemnify, defend, and hold harmless the other party from and against third-party claims arising from the indemnifying party's breach of this agreement, negligence, or willful misconduct."
  • Scope the contractor's indemnification to their deliverables. "Contractor indemnifies Client against claims that the deliverables infringe a third party's intellectual property rights, provided the deliverables were not modified by Client."
  • Scope the client's indemnification to their materials. "Client indemnifies Contractor against claims arising from Client-provided materials, content, or instructions that Contractor used in creating the deliverables."
  • Require advance approval of settlements. "The indemnifying party shall not settle any claim without the indemnified party's prior written consent, not to be unreasonably withheld."
  • Cross-reference the liability cap exception. Indemnification obligations are typically carved out from the limitation of liability cap — make this explicit.
