Exclusivity.
The clause that asks whether you can work for both sides.

Can you do SEO for a law firm and their direct competitor at the same time? Can a consultant advising on a merger also advise the other side? Exclusivity clauses answer this — and they should always be priced accordingly.

What it means

In plain English.

An exclusivity clause restricts the provider from working with the client's competitors during the engagement. It's a promise that the strategic value, proprietary information, and specific expertise deployed for this client won't simultaneously be deployed against them. It has a direct cost to the provider — and that cost should be reflected in the price.

Plain English definition

An exclusivity clause says: while you're working for this client, you can't work for their direct competitors in a defined market or category. It typically has a defined duration (the length of the engagement), a defined scope (what counts as a competitor), and is priced into the contract.

When you need it

When a client shares sensitive competitive strategy, market positioning, or proprietary information, they may legitimately need exclusivity. When a provider gives up market opportunities by taking exclusivity, they should be compensated. Both sides need to understand what they're agreeing to and why.

What happens without it

Without an exclusivity clause, the provider is free to work with the client's competitors simultaneously. The client has no contractual recourse if the consultant advising their growth strategy is also advising their largest competitor on the same market. Clients assume exclusivity; the contract should make it explicit — or explicitly deny it.

Why it's missed

Both sides assume something different about concurrent work.

Why missed

The client assumes exclusivity by default

Many clients assume that hiring a consultant or agency means they won't be serving direct competitors. The provider assumes the opposite — they can serve whoever they want unless restricted. This mismatch creates conflict when the client discovers the provider is working with a competitor.

Why missed

Pricing implications aren't understood

Exclusivity has a real cost to the provider — they're giving up market opportunities. Many providers accept exclusivity informally without charging for it, then resent the restriction. If exclusivity is required, it needs a price premium.

Why missed

"Competitor" isn't defined

What counts as a competitor? A direct competitor in the same city? A company in the same broad industry? Without a definition, both parties will have different interpretations — and the dispute starts from the first concurrent client the provider takes.

What can go wrong

The downstream consequences.

Confidentiality risk to the client

Provider working for two direct competitors receives confidential strategy from both. Without exclusivity, there's no contractual obligation to decline the competing work. Both clients' strategies are now accessible to the same provider.

Lost opportunities without compensation

Provider accepts informal exclusivity — turns down competing work to maintain the relationship. Engagement ends. Provider has declined revenue that should have been compensated in the contract. If it isn't written, it isn't paid.

Relationship breakdown mid-engagement

Client discovers provider is working with their competitor. Assumes breach of an unwritten exclusivity expectation. Relationship deteriorates. Without a clause that says exclusivity was or wasn't included, neither side can point to what was actually agreed.

Overly broad exclusivity strangling the business

Provider accepts an exclusivity clause with an overly broad definition of competitor. Suddenly they can't work with most of their target market. A $20,000 contract with exclusive rights to an entire industry is not a good deal unless it's priced as such.

Red-flag wording

Exclusivity language that creates problems.

These formulations appear in client-drafted agreements. Each one either creates uncompensated restriction or leaves the scope dangerously undefined.

Red flag

"Shall not work with any competitor"

No definition of competitor. Could mean any company in the same broad industry globally. Without a specific definition, this clause is both unenforceable and dangerously broad.

Red flag

"Exclusivity included at no additional cost"

Exclusivity always has a cost. If it's included "at no additional cost," either the overall price isn't sufficient or the exclusivity isn't real — and it won't be honored if a better competing client comes along.

Red flag

"Competitors as determined by client"

This gives the client the power to expand the definition of competitor at will. Everything becomes a competitor. The provider can be effectively locked out of their entire market.

Red flag

"For the duration of the agreement and thereafter"

"Thereafter" without a defined end date converts exclusivity into a permanent non-compete. Courts will void this language. If post-engagement exclusivity is intended, use non-solicitation with a defined duration instead.

Safer pattern

An exclusivity clause that's fair, defined, and priced.

A well-drafted exclusivity clause names the specific competitors or categories that are restricted, sets a defined duration (matching the engagement), charges an appropriate exclusivity premium, and gives both parties an out if the scope proves commercially unreasonable.

The pattern

  • Define the competitor category specifically. "During this engagement, Contractor shall not provide [the same type of services] to [list specific named competitors OR companies that primarily compete in the market defined as: X]."
  • Limit duration to the engagement period. "This exclusivity obligation applies only during the term of this agreement. No post-engagement exclusivity applies unless separately agreed in writing."
  • Price it explicitly. "The total fee includes an exclusivity premium of $[X], reflecting Contractor's agreement to decline competing work during the engagement period."
  • Create a market re-entry mechanism. "If Client wishes to extend exclusivity beyond the engagement term, Client may do so at a fee of $[X] per additional month, payable in advance."
  • Address pre-existing clients. "This exclusivity does not apply to [named pre-existing clients of Contractor], with whom work was underway prior to this agreement."
