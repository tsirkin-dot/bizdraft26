Approval timeline.
The clause that keeps silence from costing you.

You delivered the work. The client went quiet. Without an approval timeline clause, their silence has no contractual consequence — and your project sits in limbo, unpaid.

What it means

In plain English.

An approval timeline clause gives the client a specific window to review and respond to each deliverable. If they respond with approved feedback, the revision cycle begins. If they respond with approval, the payment trigger fires. If they don't respond at all — deemed acceptance: the deliverable is considered accepted as delivered.

Plain English definition

The approval timeline clause specifies how long the client has to review and respond to each deliverable — typically 5–10 business days. It also says what happens if they don't respond within that window: the deliverable is deemed accepted, and any payment tied to that acceptance comes due.

When you need it

Whenever your project involves a review-and-approval cycle, you need an approval timeline. Design projects, writing, development, consulting reports — any engagement where the client reviews your work before you proceed or before you invoice.

What happens without it

Without an approval timeline, client silence has no contractual consequence. They can sit on a deliverable indefinitely, blocking the next phase, holding up payment, and creating timeline drift that is entirely outside your control.

Why it's missed

Most contracts only constrain the provider, not the client.

Why missed

It feels presumptuous

Telling a client "you have 5 days to respond or it's deemed approved" sounds aggressive before the project starts. But without it, the client has no incentive to respond promptly — and your timeline slides.

Why missed

The contract only controls the provider

Most service agreements detail what the provider must do and when — but say nothing about client obligations. The approval timeline clause is one of the few clauses that creates a contractual obligation on the client side.

Why missed

"They'll respond when they're ready"

Clients are busy. Without a deadline, "when they're ready" becomes "when the project is no longer urgent" — which can be months. A defined window respects both parties' time.

What can go wrong

The downstream consequences.

Projects stall indefinitely

You deliver draft one. Client takes three weeks to respond. You deliver draft two. Client takes two more weeks. A 6-week project becomes 4 months. Your calendar is blocked. Other work suffers.

Payment delays

Final payment is tied to final approval. Client doesn't approve — not because they're unhappy, but because they're busy, distracted, or disorganized. Invoice sits unpaid. No mechanism to force it forward.

Retroactive change requests

Client approved a design in week two, then three weeks later decides they want changes. Without a defined approval process, they claim they never formally approved anything. You're back to square one.

Timeline liability

You missed the project deadline — because the client missed their review windows. But without a clause recording client delays, you bear the responsibility for the missed date.

Red-flag wording

Approval language that gives the client unlimited time.

These phrases are common. Each one removes the timeline and gives client silence no consequence.

Red flag

"Upon client approval"

No timeline. No deemed-acceptance provision. The client approves whenever — or never. Payment and next phases wait indefinitely.

Red flag

"When client is satisfied"

Satisfaction is subjective and undefined. No clock, no mechanism. This phrase puts approval entirely in client hands with no deadline.

Red flag

"Feedback provided at client's convenience"

This language explicitly removes any timing obligation. You wrote the problem into the contract.

Red flag

"Client will review in a timely manner"

"Timely" is not a number. It has no enforceability. Courts interpret it differently in every jurisdiction.

Red flag

"Mutually agreed upon approval"

Mutual agreement requires two parties. If the client disagrees, approval never happens. No timeline, no fallback.

Safer pattern

An approval timeline clause that moves things forward.

A strong approval timeline clause combines a specific response window, a deemed-acceptance provision, and a clause that extends project deadlines when client delays cause timeline drift. Together these three provisions ensure client silence never benefits the client.

BizDraft includes approval timeline terms calibrated to your delivery cycle, with deemed-acceptance windows appropriate to the size and complexity of each deliverable type.

The pattern

  • Set the response window. "Client has 5 business days from the date of delivery to submit written feedback or approval for each deliverable."
  • Define deemed acceptance. "Failure to respond within this window constitutes acceptance of the deliverable as delivered. No further revisions will be provided on accepted deliverables."
  • Extend your deadlines for client delay. "Project deadlines are extended by one business day for each business day Client exceeds the review window, without penalty to Contractor."
  • Define what valid feedback looks like. "Feedback must identify specific objections to specific deliverables. General dissatisfaction or preference changes do not constitute valid rejection."
  • Tie approval to payment. "Deemed or actual acceptance of a deliverable triggers any payment due at that milestone per the payment schedule in Section [X]."
