Client responsibilities.
The clause that puts obligations on both sides.

The project stalled because the client didn't provide login credentials. Or brand assets. Or a decision. Without a client responsibilities clause, you bear the timeline consequences of their delays.

What it means

In plain English.

A client responsibilities clause lists the specific things the client must provide — on time and in usable form — for the project to proceed as contracted. It creates contractual obligations on the client side, not just the provider side, and specifies what happens when those obligations aren't met: timeline extends, not your fault.

Plain English definition

The clause says: the provider will deliver on the agreed timeline IF the client provides [list of inputs] on the agreed schedule. If the client doesn't provide those inputs on time, deadlines shift by the same amount, and the provider isn't in breach for the resulting delay.

When you need it

Any project where you need something from the client to proceed: content, brand assets, system access, approvals, decisions, sign-offs, subject matter expertise, or review feedback. In short: every project. The provider is never the only active party in a project.

What happens without it

Without a client responsibilities clause, your timeline obligations are unconditional. If the client doesn't provide what you need, you're still expected to hit the deadline. When you miss it because of their delay, you're in technical breach — with no contractual protection.

Why it's missed

Most contracts only bind the provider. The client gets away with everything.

Why missed

Contracts are written for the provider's obligations

Service agreements focus on what the provider will deliver, when, and at what quality. Client obligations are an afterthought — or omitted entirely. Both parties have obligations; both should be in the contract.

Why missed

It feels accusatory to list what the client needs to do

Telling a client "you need to provide X by [date]" can feel like you're expecting them to fail. But framing it as mutual obligations — both parties working toward the deliverable — makes it professional, not accusatory.

Why missed

The inputs aren't fully known at signing

At signing, the exact list of client inputs may not be determined. The answer isn't to omit the clause — it's to define the categories of inputs required and specify that a detailed input list will be agreed within [X] days of signing.

What can go wrong

The downstream consequences.

Deadline missed for client reasons

Client takes two weeks to provide brand assets. Project launches two weeks late. Client claims you missed the contractual deadline. Without a clause tying your timeline to client inputs, their delay becomes your breach.

Scope expanding through inputs

Client provides assets that require significant rework to be usable. Client expects this rework to be included. Without a clause defining what "usable assets" means and who bears rework cost, the scope expands silently.

Decision delays blocking progress

The project requires a key stakeholder decision before proceeding. Stakeholder is unavailable for three weeks. Project stalls. Without a client responsibilities clause that makes timely decisions a client obligation, you're stuck waiting with no contractual recourse.

Access not provided

Developer needs access to client's CMS, database, and hosting account. Client's IT department takes two weeks to provision access. Without a clause requiring access by a specific date, the development timeline is blown through no fault of the developer.

Red-flag wording

Contracts that omit or weaken client obligations.

These formulations — and their absence — are the most common failures in service agreements.

Red flag

No client responsibilities section at all

The most common failure. The contract describes what the provider does but says nothing about what the client must provide. All obligations are one-sided. All delays are the provider's problem.

Red flag

"Client will cooperate as needed"

Not specific, not timely, not enforceable. "Cooperate as needed" creates no concrete obligation and no mechanism for documenting non-cooperation.

Red flag

"Timeline assumes client promptness"

An assumption isn't a clause. The contract should specify what "prompt" means and what happens when the client isn't.

Red flag

"Provider responsible for meeting all deadlines"

Unconditional deadline responsibility with no mention of client input obligations. Provider bears the full risk of timeline, even where client delays are the cause.

Safer pattern

A client responsibilities clause with teeth.

A strong client responsibilities clause is specific (named inputs, not vague cooperation), timed (by when), and consequential (client delay extends provider timeline by equal amount). It treats the project as a two-party collaboration where both sides have obligations.

The pattern

  • List specific inputs. "Client agrees to provide the following by the dates specified: (a) brand guidelines and logo files by [date]; (b) written content for all pages by [date]; (c) access credentials to [systems] by [date]; (d) a designated point of contact who can make decisions within 48 hours."
  • Define usable quality. "All materials must be in the formats specified in Exhibit A and of sufficient quality for the intended use. Materials requiring significant rework may require a change order."
  • Make timeline extensions automatic for client delays. "If Client fails to provide required inputs within 3 business days of the scheduled date, Contractor's delivery dates are automatically extended by the same number of days, without penalty."
  • Require a single point of contact. "Client shall designate a single primary contact with authority to make project decisions. Contractor is not responsible for delays caused by unclear or conflicting instructions from multiple stakeholders."
  • Address access provision. "Client shall provision all required system access within 5 business days of signing. Work requiring this access will not begin until access is provided; related deadlines adjust accordingly."
