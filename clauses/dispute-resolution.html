Dispute resolution.
The clause that decides the cost of a fight.

Whether a dispute costs $5,000 or $150,000 to resolve often comes down to which clause is — or isn't — in the contract. Court is expensive. Arbitration is faster. Mediation first can prevent both.

What it means

In plain English.

A dispute resolution clause specifies the process for resolving disagreements that the parties can't resolve themselves. The three main options are: litigation (court), arbitration (a private judge whose decision is binding), and mediation (a facilitator who helps the parties settle, without binding authority). Most well-drafted clauses require mediation first, then arbitration if mediation fails.

Plain English definition

The clause says: if we have a disagreement we can't resolve, here's the process we follow. It specifies who decides (an arbitrator, a judge), where (which state, which venue), by which rules (AAA, JAMS, state court rules), and who pays the fees of the process.

When you need it

Every service agreement needs a dispute resolution clause. Without one, either party can file in their home state court — which may be across the country from the other. The clause controls where and how disputes are heard, which matters enormously to cost and outcome.

What happens without it

Without a dispute resolution clause, either party can file in any court with jurisdiction. The client may sue you in their home state. You may have to hire a local attorney, travel for hearings, and navigate unfamiliar local court rules. This asymmetry often makes defending a legitimate claim economically irrational.

Why it's missed

Disputes feel hypothetical until you're in one.

Why missed

Small contracts feel too small to dispute

A $3,000 project doesn't feel like something that would end up in arbitration. But the legal cost of defending even a small claim can exceed the project value. A well-placed dispute resolution clause makes the economics of litigation unattractive to both parties.

Why missed

Arbitration vs. litigation isn't understood

Most business owners don't know the practical differences. Arbitration is typically faster, less formal, and more private than court — but the fees can be high for small claims. For most small business disputes, mandatory mediation first is the most cost-effective path.

Why missed

Venue seems like a formality

Specifying "disputes shall be resolved in [your state]" sounds bureaucratic until a client in a different state files against you in their local court. At that point, the venue clause would have saved significant cost and inconvenience.

What can go wrong

The downstream consequences.

Sued in the wrong state

Client files in their home state. You need to hire an out-of-state attorney, potentially travel for hearings, and deal with unfamiliar local rules. A $5,000 dispute becomes a $20,000 defense cost — making settlement cheaper than winning.

No pre-litigation cooling-off period

Without a mandatory mediation or negotiation requirement, parties escalate to lawyers immediately. Most commercial disputes settle for a fraction of the litigation cost — if the parties are required to try before suing.

Public proceedings

Court proceedings are public record. A dispute over a $10,000 project that contains confidential client information may result in sensitive details being publicly disclosed. Arbitration is private; litigation is not.

Costly small claims

Without a clause directing small disputes to appropriate forums, a $2,000 invoice dispute may end up in full civil court rather than small claims. Clause: "Claims under $10,000 may be brought in small claims court in [your county]."

Red-flag wording

Dispute resolution language that works against you.

These formulations are one-sided or incomplete. Each one creates a situation where the less-resourced party is at a significant disadvantage.

Red flag

"Disputes resolved in client's home jurisdiction"

One-sided venue provision. If the client is in a different state, you're defending claims in a foreign court. Never accept without negotiating to your home state or a neutral venue.

Red flag

"Binding arbitration under AAA rules, fees split equally"

AAA arbitration for a $5,000 dispute can cost $3,000–8,000 in fees alone. Equal fee splitting may make pursuing a legitimate small claim economically irrational. A small claims court carve-out is essential.

Red flag

"Loser pays all attorney's fees"

One-way fee shifting can be appropriate — but bilateral loser-pays creates a chilling effect on legitimate claims. Parties may not pursue valid claims because the downside risk is too high.

Red flag

"No dispute resolution clause at all"

Silence means the parties default to state court rules and jurisdiction — which may favor whoever files first and wherever they file. The absence of a clause is an invitation to choose the forum strategically.

Safer pattern

A dispute resolution clause that makes fighting expensive enough to avoid.

The most effective dispute resolution clauses for small businesses use a three-step approach: informal negotiation first (cheap), mediation second (a few hundred dollars), arbitration or small claims third (more costly but still cheaper than full litigation). Each step creates an off-ramp before the costs escalate.

The pattern

  • Require good-faith negotiation first. "The parties shall attempt to resolve any dispute through good-faith negotiation for 30 days before initiating any formal process."
  • Require mediation if negotiation fails. "If negotiation is unsuccessful, the parties shall submit the dispute to non-binding mediation before a mutually agreed mediator. Mediation costs shall be split equally."
  • Specify arbitration or small claims as the final step. "Claims under $10,000 may be brought in small claims court in [your county, your state]. Claims over $10,000 shall be resolved by binding arbitration under [AAA/JAMS] rules in [your city, your state]."
  • Name your governing law and venue. "This agreement is governed by the laws of [State]. Any proceedings shall be conducted in [your county], [State]."
  • Address attorney's fees. "Each party bears its own attorney's fees unless the arbitrator or court determines that a claim was brought frivolously, in which case fees may be awarded to the prevailing party."
