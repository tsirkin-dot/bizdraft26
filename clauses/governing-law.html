Governing law.
The clause that quietly determines the rest.

Which state's law governs the contract determines what your liability cap means, whether your non-solicitation clause is enforceable, and what "reasonable" means in every ambiguous clause.

What it means

In plain English.

A governing law clause specifies which state's law the contract will be interpreted under. Every state has its own commercial law — variations in how contracts are construed, what clauses are enforceable, and what "implied" terms exist. The governing law clause determines which version applies.

Plain English definition

"This agreement shall be governed by and construed in accordance with the laws of the State of [X]." That sentence determines which state's courts, which state's statutes, and which state's common law precedents apply to interpret and enforce every other clause in the agreement.

When you need it

Every contract between parties in different states — and even contracts between parties in the same state benefit from explicit governing law to prevent ambiguity if one party later moves. If you do business across state lines, this clause is essential.

What happens without it

Without a governing law clause, courts apply conflict-of-laws analysis to determine which state's law governs. This is unpredictable, expensive to litigate, and can result in the less favorable state's law being applied without either party intending it.

Why it's missed

It seems like boilerplate. The impact is anything but.

Why missed

It reads like fine print

Governing law clauses appear at the end of contracts, labeled "Miscellaneous" or "General Provisions." Both parties skim past them at signing — and neither fully understands the implications until they're in a dispute.

Why missed

Both parties are in the same state

When provider and client are both in the same state, governing law seems obvious. But clients move, businesses expand, and successor companies may be in different states. The clause future-proofs the agreement.

Why missed

The downstream effects aren't obvious

California won't enforce non-solicitation clauses that most other states would uphold. Texas has different non-compete law than New York. Delaware corporate law is significantly different from Montana's. The state named in this clause can change what's possible throughout the contract.

What can go wrong

The downstream consequences.

Unenforceable restrictions

Non-solicitation and non-compete clauses have radically different enforceability by state. A California governing law clause may render enforceable restrictions completely void — even if both parties are based in states where the clause would be valid.

Different implied terms

Every state has different implied contract terms — what "reasonable" means, what duties are implied, what the default rule is for ambiguous clauses. The governing law clause determines which state's implied terms fill the gaps in your agreement.

Conflict-of-laws litigation

Without a governing law clause, parties can spend tens of thousands of dollars litigating which state's law applies — before any substantive issue is even argued. This preliminary motion is entirely avoidable with a two-sentence clause.

Consumer protection variation

Consumer protection laws vary enormously by state. If a client is an individual consumer, governing law can determine whether your limitation of liability clause is valid or whether state-specific consumer protections override your contractual terms.

Red-flag wording

Governing law language that creates problems.

These formulations are common and each one creates a problem the parties don't anticipate at signing.

Red flag

"Laws of the State of California" for out-of-state providers

California has the most restrictive anti-non-compete and non-solicitation law in the country. If your agreement includes post-engagement restrictions, California governing law may void them entirely. Understand what you're agreeing to before accepting a client's boilerplate.

Red flag

"Federal law governs"

Federal law doesn't govern most commercial contracts. Federal law covers specific areas (IP, federal securities, employment discrimination) but not general contract interpretation. This clause is vague and unhelpful.

Red flag

No governing law clause at all

Without a clause, a court applies conflict-of-laws rules. The result is unpredictable and expensive to determine. Always include a governing law clause.

Red flag

"Laws of the jurisdiction where work is performed"

Work may be performed in multiple jurisdictions. This phrase creates ambiguity rather than resolving it. Name the specific state.

Safer pattern

Choosing and drafting your governing law clause.

As a general rule, providers should name their own state as the governing law — you know your state's law, your local attorneys know it, and any disputes in your home state are logistically manageable. Resist client pressure to use their state's law unless there's a compelling reason.

The pattern

  • Name your home state. "This agreement shall be governed by and construed in accordance with the laws of the State of [Your State], without regard to its conflict-of-laws principles."
  • Include the conflict-of-laws exclusion. "Without regard to its conflict-of-laws principles" prevents a court from applying another state's law even if your state's conflict rules would point there.
  • Pair with a venue clause. "Any legal proceedings arising under this agreement shall be conducted in [your county], [your state]." Governing law and venue work together — you need both.
  • Check your restrictive covenants against your chosen state. Before finalizing, verify that your non-solicitation or confidentiality terms are enforceable under the state you've chosen. If you're using California law, understand the limitations.
  • Match with your dispute resolution clause. Governing law and dispute resolution should reference the same state and be consistent about forum.
