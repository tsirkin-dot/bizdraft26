Confidentiality.
The clause that keeps business information inside.

Business plans, pricing, customer lists, trade secrets — information shared in the course of a business relationship needs a contractual obligation to stay there. Without one, it's legally shareable.

What it means

In plain English.

A confidentiality clause — also called an NDA provision when it's a standalone agreement — creates a contractual obligation for the recipient of confidential information to keep it secret and use it only for the purposes of the engagement. It defines what counts as confidential, how long the obligation lasts, and what the exclusions are.

Plain English definition

The confidentiality clause says: "Information you receive about my business in connection with this project is secret. You can use it for this project only. You can't share it with others, use it for other purposes, or disclose it after the project ends." Both parties typically have obligations.

When you need it

Any engagement where either party shares sensitive business information: financial data, pricing, client lists, product plans, strategies, trade secrets, or proprietary processes. Also: when a client shares their data and you need to protect yourself from liability if it leaks.

What happens without it

Without a confidentiality clause, there is no legal obligation to protect information shared in the course of the engagement. Information a client shares with their contractor can be shared with the contractor's other clients, used for the contractor's own purposes, or disclosed publicly.

Why it's missed

It's treated as an extra document, not a standard clause.

Why missed

NDAs feel like a separate thing

Many businesses sign a standalone NDA before discussions, then forget to include confidentiality terms in the actual service contract. A separate NDA and a contract clause can have different definitions and durations — which creates gaps.

Why missed

Small engagements feel low-risk

"We're just doing a logo" — but the client shared their go-to-market strategy in the brief. That's confidential information, and now you have no obligation to protect it. The size of the engagement doesn't determine the sensitivity of the information shared.

Why missed

Mutual vs. one-way confusion

Business owners assume they need to protect the client's information, but not vice versa. The contractor's pricing, methods, and processes are also confidential. Mutual confidentiality protects both parties.

What can go wrong

The downstream consequences.

Trade secret disclosure

A contractor shares a client's pricing model with a competitor — not maliciously, just in conversation. Without a confidentiality obligation, there's no breach of contract, only potentially a trade secret misappropriation claim, which is much harder to prove.

Client data liability

A contractor handles customer data. Without confidentiality and data handling terms, if that data leaks, the contractor has no contractual limitation on their liability. The client can claim the full value of the breach.

Portfolio breach

A contractor publishes client work publicly before launch. The client's product is revealed ahead of their announcement. No confidentiality clause, no breach of contract. The client's competitive advantage is gone.

Reverse disclosure

The client shares the contractor's proprietary methods, pricing, or processes with a competitor. Without mutual confidentiality, there's no contractual obligation on the client's side either.

Red-flag wording

Confidentiality language that doesn't protect.

These formulations appear in many contracts. Each one has a gap that disclosure walks through.

Red flag

"Information marked confidential"

Requires the disclosing party to physically mark every document. Sensitive information shared verbally, via email, or in a meeting is not protected. In practice, nothing gets marked.

Red flag

"Confidential during the project"

The obligation expires when the project ends. Trade secrets and business information don't lose their sensitivity at project completion. Post-termination confidentiality is essential.

Red flag

"Shall not share with unauthorized parties"

Who authorized whom? Without a definition of authorized parties, this phrase is unenforceable. The other party can authorize their entire team and anyone they do business with.

Red flag

"Best efforts to maintain confidentiality"

"Best efforts" is a low bar and a high dispute. It invites argument about what effort was reasonable. Use "shall not disclose" — an absolute obligation — not a best-efforts qualifier.

Safer pattern

A confidentiality clause that actually protects.

A strong confidentiality clause defines confidential information broadly (not just marked documents), specifies the permitted uses, limits disclosure to need-to-know personnel, survives termination for a defined period, and carves out standard exclusions like information already public or independently developed.

The pattern

  • Define confidential information broadly. "Confidential information means all non-public business, technical, financial, or operational information shared by either party in connection with this agreement, whether or not marked confidential."
  • Limit permitted use. "Recipient may use confidential information only for the purposes of this engagement and for no other purpose."
  • Limit disclosure. "Recipient shall not disclose confidential information to third parties. Recipient may share it with employees or contractors who need it for the engagement and who are bound by equivalent confidentiality obligations."
  • Survive termination. "This confidentiality obligation survives termination of this agreement for 3 years."
  • Include standard exclusions. "This clause does not apply to information that: (a) was publicly known at disclosure; (b) was already known to recipient; (c) was independently developed; or (d) must be disclosed by law."
