Termination.
The clause that decides how cleanly you can exit.

Every business relationship ends eventually. A termination clause determines whether the ending is orderly or contentious — and whether you're paid for the work you've already done.

What it means

In plain English.

A termination clause specifies who can end the agreement, under what circumstances, with how much notice, and what happens afterward — who owes what, who returns what, and which clauses survive the end of the relationship.

Plain English definition

Termination clauses come in two types: termination for cause (one party breached something material) and termination for convenience (either party can exit without a stated reason, typically with notice). Both need to be addressed, and both have different payment consequences.

When you need it

Every service agreement, retainer, and long-term engagement needs a termination clause. Without one, a contract that has no natural end date may legally bind both parties until one of them breaches — which is an expensive way to exit.

What happens without it

Without a termination clause, ending a relationship requires either mutual agreement or a material breach. If a client simply stops responding or paying, you may have to choose between continuing work (unpaid) or breaching the contract yourself to exit.

Why it's missed

Nobody wants to plan for the relationship ending before it starts.

Why missed

It feels pessimistic

Writing termination terms feels like planning for failure. But the termination clause isn't evidence of distrust — it's evidence of professionalism. Every well-drafted commercial contract has one.

Why missed

Short projects feel fine without it

A two-week project seems unlikely to need a termination clause. But projects stall, clients become unresponsive, payments stop — at any project length, an exit mechanism protects both parties.

Why missed

Termination for cause vs. convenience isn't understood

Most business owners don't know the difference. Without both types, clients can sometimes exit without paying for completed work by claiming there was no agreed way to end the contract without cause.

What can go wrong

The downstream consequences.

Locked in with a bad client

Without termination for convenience, you may have no contractual right to exit a relationship that's become untenable — unless you can prove material breach. Bad clients know this and use it to hold providers hostage.

Unpaid work at exit

Client terminates. You've done three weeks of work under a milestone-based contract where the milestone hasn't been reached. Without language covering payment for pre-termination work, you may receive nothing for those three weeks.

IP and deliverable limbo

Agreement ends. Who owns the half-finished work? Can the client use it? Can you? Without termination consequences spelled out, the answer is disputed — and disputes are expensive.

Surviving clause confusion

Confidentiality, IP, non-solicitation — these need to survive termination. But if the contract says termination ends "all obligations," those protections may disappear. Specify which clauses survive.

Red-flag wording

Termination language that traps you in or leaves you unpaid.

These formulations are common. Each one creates a problem at the moment you need the clause to work.

Red flag

"Terminable only for cause"

If there's no termination for convenience right, exiting requires proving a breach. Good relationships end for business reasons that aren't breaches. You need an exit that doesn't require fault.

Red flag

"Immediate termination"

Zero notice. You complete work on Tuesday, client terminates Wednesday morning before you invoice. Without a notice period and pro-rata payment clause, completed work may not be compensable.

Red flag

"All obligations cease upon termination"

This language terminates confidentiality, IP protections, payment obligations, and non-solicitation simultaneously. Almost nothing should survive this clause as written.

Red flag

"No compensation owed if terminated by client"

One-sided. Client can exit at any time, owe nothing for work already done. Always resist this language and replace it with pro-rata payment for work completed to date.

Safer pattern

A termination clause that protects the exit.

A well-drafted termination clause covers both for-cause and for-convenience scenarios, requires notice for convenience terminations, specifies payment for work done before termination, addresses deliverable and IP disposition, and lists the clauses that survive.

The pattern

  • Allow termination for convenience with notice. "Either party may terminate this agreement for any reason with 14 days written notice."
  • Define termination for cause. "Either party may terminate immediately upon written notice if the other party materially breaches this agreement and fails to cure the breach within 7 days of written notice."
  • Ensure payment for pre-termination work. "Upon termination for any reason, Client shall pay Contractor for all work completed through the termination date at the pro-rata rate of the total project fee."
  • Address deliverables. "Upon payment of all amounts due, Contractor will deliver all completed work product. Incomplete work product is retained by Contractor."
  • List surviving clauses. "Sections [Confidentiality, IP, Limitation of Liability, Non-Solicitation] survive termination of this agreement."
