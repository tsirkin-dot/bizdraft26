Late payment.
The clause that makes tardiness expensive.

An invoice without consequences is a suggestion. A late payment clause with interest, fees, and work suspension rights is a contract.

What it means

In plain English.

A late payment clause specifies exactly what happens after the payment due date passes: an interest rate that accrues on the unpaid balance, a flat late fee, and — most importantly — your right to suspend work until the account is current. Without it, late payment has no cost to the client.

Plain English definition

If an invoice isn't paid by the due date, the clause kicks in automatically. A typical structure: a flat fee on the first day late (say, $50), then a daily or monthly interest rate (1.5% per month is common) on the unpaid balance until paid in full.

When you need it

Any contract where payment isn't collected at the time of service — which means most service agreements, consulting contracts, and freelance arrangements. If money moves after work is delivered, you need a late payment clause.

What happens without it

Without a late payment clause, a client who pays 45 days late has cost you nothing extra. There's no financial incentive to pay on time. Your best tool is a polite email — which rarely works as well as a contractual obligation to pay interest.

Why it's missed

It feels aggressive. So most people skip it.

Why missed

It signals distrust

Business owners assume that mentioning late fees implies they expect the client to pay late — which feels insulting. But the clause is for the client who actually is late, not the one who pays on time.

Why missed

The interest rate feels arbitrary

Most freelancers don't know what rate to use. 1.5% per month? 18% annually? The uncertainty leads to omitting the clause rather than picking a rate.

Why missed

Suspension feels like nuclear

The right to suspend work is the clause's most powerful provision — and the most intimidating to include. But it's also the one that gets clients to pay fastest when invoked.

What can go wrong

The downstream consequences.

Chronic late payment

Clients learn what is and isn't enforced. If late payment has no cost, it becomes the pattern. Net-30 becomes net-60 because nothing happens at day 31.

Continuing to work without pay

Without a work suspension right, you have no lever when payment is overdue. You keep working, keep delivering, and keep accumulating unpaid invoices with no mechanism to stop.

Uncollectable debt

The longer an invoice ages, the harder it is to collect. Late payment interest creates urgency. Without it, there's no financial pressure to pay an old invoice instead of a new one.

Collections cost more than the debt

Taking a $2,000 invoice to small claims court or hiring a collections agency eats most of the recovery. A clause that prevents the invoice from aging avoids this entirely.

Red-flag wording

Phrases that look like late payment terms but aren't.

These formulations give the impression of a late payment clause while providing no real enforcement mechanism.

Red flag

"Payment expected promptly"

This is social pressure, not a contract term. No date, no consequence, no enforceability.

Red flag

"Late fees may apply"

"May apply" is discretionary. If the fee is optional, the client assumes it won't be applied and ignores the clause.

Red flag

"Interest at the legal rate"

Legal interest rates vary wildly by state and are often very low. Specifying an actual rate (e.g. 1.5% per month) is far more effective.

Red flag

"Work may be paused at contractor's discretion"

Vague discretionary language creates disputes about whether the right was exercised properly. Better: "Contractor may suspend all work without notice if payment is overdue by more than 10 days."

Red flag

"Reasonable late charges"

Whose reasonable? This phrase is litigated, not enforced. Name the number.

Safer pattern

What an enforceable late payment clause looks like.

The clause needs three components: a trigger (the due date), a consequence (the fee or interest rate), and the suspension right. All three need to be specific, not discretionary.

The pattern

  • Name the grace period. "Invoices unpaid within 7 days of the due date are subject to a late fee of $75, applied automatically."
  • Specify the interest rate. "Interest accrues at 1.5% per month (18% per annum) on any unpaid balance beginning on the due date."
  • Include the suspension right. "Contractor may suspend all work and withhold deliverables if any invoice is overdue by more than 10 business days, without liability to Client for resulting delays."
  • Address resumed work conditions. "Work will resume within 3 business days of receipt of all overdue amounts including accrued interest."
  • Cover collection costs. "Client is responsible for all reasonable costs of collection, including attorney's fees, if legal action becomes necessary."
