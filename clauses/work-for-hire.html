Work for hire.
The doctrine that makes the client the author.

Work for hire isn't just a clause — it's a specific legal doctrine under federal copyright law. If it applies, the client is the legal author from day one. If it doesn't, they need an assignment instead.

What it means

In plain English.

Under the US Copyright Act, "work made for hire" is a specific legal category. It applies automatically to work created by employees. For independent contractors, it only applies to certain categories of work — AND only if a signed written agreement says it's work for hire before the work is created.

Plain English definition

Work for hire makes the client the legal author — not just the owner — of the work. The copyright vests in the client from creation, with a 95-year term rather than the creator's life plus 70 years. It also means the creator has no termination rights to reclaim the work after 35 years.

When it applies to contractors

Work for hire applies to independent contractors ONLY for work that falls into one of nine statutory categories (contributions to collective works, translations, instructional texts, tests, supplementary works, compilations, audiovisual works, atlases, and motion pictures) AND only with a signed written agreement.

What happens without the written agreement

If the work qualifies under one of the nine categories but there's no signed written agreement, work for hire doesn't apply. The creator retains copyright. The client should use a copyright assignment clause instead — which is valid for any type of work.

Why it's missed

Everyone uses the phrase. Almost no one knows the doctrine.

Why missed

The phrase is everywhere

"This is a work for hire" appears in contracts for every type of creative service. Most of the time it's legally meaningless because the work doesn't fall into one of the nine statutory categories and/or the agreement was signed after the work was created.

Why missed

The nine-category limit is unknown

Most business owners and many lawyers don't know that work for hire for contractors only applies to nine specific types of work. Logo design, web design, and most freelance deliverables are NOT in those nine categories. The clause is void if the work type doesn't qualify.

Why missed

The timing requirement is overlooked

For work for hire to apply to a contractor, the written agreement must be signed BEFORE the work is created. A retroactive "this was work for hire" clause is ineffective. If the work is already done, use an assignment instead.

What can go wrong

The downstream consequences.

The clause is void and you don't know it

The contract says "work for hire." The work is logo design. Logo design is not in the nine categories. The work-for-hire clause is legally meaningless — and the client believes they own what they don't.

The 35-year termination right

Copyright assignments (as opposed to true work for hire) can be terminated by the creator 35 years later. If a client receives an assignment instead of true work for hire, they may face a termination claim decades later. For major brands, this is significant.

Employment classification risk

Work for hire for employees is automatic — but relying on work-for-hire language in a freelance contract may be used as evidence of an employment relationship, creating tax and benefits liability for the client.

No fallback assignment

If the work-for-hire clause fails, is there a backup assignment clause? Most contracts that rely on "work for hire" don't have one. If the doctrine doesn't apply, the client is left with an implied license at best.

Red-flag wording

Work for hire language that doesn't work.

These phrases are common. Each one is either ineffective or legally incorrect for the work it's supposed to cover.

Red flag

"All work is work for hire"

If any of the work doesn't fall into the nine statutory categories, that portion of the clause is void. Blanket work-for-hire language is only effective for qualifying work types.

Red flag

"Retroactively designated as work for hire"

Work for hire cannot be retroactively designated. It must be agreed before creation. This clause is legally ineffective.

Red flag

"Contractor acknowledges work is work for hire"

An acknowledgment doesn't make it so. Work for hire has a legal definition. Parties cannot make something work for hire by agreeing it is, if it doesn't meet the statutory criteria.

Red flag

"Logo/design is work for hire"

Logo design and most graphic design work does not fall into the nine statutory categories. This clause is void for those work types. An assignment is the correct mechanism.

Safer pattern

The right way to handle copyright transfer for contractors.

For most freelance and contractor work, a copyright assignment is more reliable than work-for-hire language. An assignment transfers copyright for any type of work and doesn't depend on the nine-category limitation. Best practice is to include both: work for hire where it applies, and an assignment as a fallback for everything else.

BizDraft uses assignment language as the default IP transfer mechanism for contractor work, with work-for-hire language added only where it legally applies.

The pattern

  • Use assignment as the primary mechanism. "Contractor hereby assigns to Client all right, title, and interest in the deliverables, including copyright, effective upon receipt of full payment."
  • Add work-for-hire language only for qualifying work. "To the extent any deliverable qualifies as a work made for hire under 17 U.S.C. § 101, it shall be so treated."
  • Include a fallback. "To the extent any deliverable does not qualify as work made for hire, Contractor hereby assigns all copyright in such deliverable to Client."
  • Reserve background IP. "The assignment does not include any pre-existing tools, templates, or methods Contractor brings to the engagement."
  • Make the agreement prior to creation. For work-for-hire language to have any effect, the signed agreement must precede the creation of the work. Sign before starting.
