Intellectual property.
The clause that decides who owns what you built.

Who owns the logo you designed? The code you wrote? The strategy you developed? Without a clear IP clause, both sides have a claim — and disputes about creative work ownership are expensive to resolve.

What it means

In plain English.

An intellectual property clause defines who owns the copyright in the finished deliverables, the work in progress, any underlying tools or frameworks used, and any background IP the provider brings to the engagement. It also specifies when ownership transfers — typically upon payment in full.

Plain English definition

By default under US copyright law, the creator owns what they create. To give the client ownership, the contract must explicitly assign it. Without an assignment clause, the client gets a license to use the work — but the provider retains the copyright.

When you need it

Every creative services contract. Design, writing, software development, photography, video, marketing strategy, brand work. If you create something, the IP clause determines what the client can do with it and what you retain.

What happens without it

Without an IP clause, both sides may believe they own the work. The client assumes they own what they paid for. The provider assumes they retain what they created. Both assumptions are legally wrong in different ways, and litigation is the only resolution.

Why it's missed

Most people assume payment equals ownership. It doesn't.

Why missed

The payment assumption

"I paid for it, I own it." This is not how US copyright law works. Payment gives you a license, not ownership, unless the contract explicitly assigns the copyright. Both sides are often shocked to learn this.

Why missed

The IP issue feels theoretical

At signing, ownership seems irrelevant. The project hasn't started. The dispute seems hypothetical. The IP clause becomes critical the moment the relationship sours, someone sells the business, or someone wants to reuse the work.

Why missed

The background IP problem

Providers bring existing tools, frameworks, and methods to every project. Without a clause reserving background IP, clients may claim ownership of those underlying assets. The clause protects you from accidentally giving away more than the deliverable.

What can go wrong

The downstream consequences.

Client can't register or enforce

Without an explicit assignment, the client can't register the copyright in the US Copyright Office. They can't enforce it against infringers. They have an implied license, but not ownership.

Provider can't reuse methods

If the IP clause is too broad, the provider accidentally assigns their background systems, templates, or processes to the client. They can't reuse their own methods on the next project without potentially infringing the client's rights.

Ownership transfers without payment

If the clause doesn't tie IP transfer to full payment, the client may receive full ownership before the final invoice is paid — and then have no incentive to pay.

Work-in-progress disputes

Client cancels mid-project. What happens to the half-finished work? Without clarity, both sides claim it. Provider withholds it. Client demands it. The dispute is expensive and the incomplete work is worth little to either party.

Red-flag wording

IP language that creates more problems than it solves.

These formulations appear constantly in service contracts. Each one creates ambiguity that costs money to resolve.

Red flag

"All rights reserved to client"

Blanket assignments include background IP, tools, methods, and framework code. The provider may have just assigned assets they need to continue working with any client.

Red flag

"Client owns upon payment"

Vague: payment of what? Which invoice? The deposit, or the final balance? Without specifying "payment in full," partial payment might trigger full ownership.

Red flag

"Provider retains the right to use for portfolio"

A portfolio right is a license carve-out — but it's often not specific enough. Can you show it publicly? By name? In case studies? The scope of the portfolio right needs to be defined.

Red flag

"Joint ownership of deliverables"

Joint ownership under US law is complicated. Either party can license the work to a third party without the other's consent. Joint ownership often satisfies neither party and creates ongoing entanglement.

Safer pattern

An IP clause that protects both sides.

A well-drafted IP clause has three layers: what the client receives (full assignment of specific deliverables upon payment in full), what the provider retains (background IP, tools, general methods), and what the provider can do with the work afterward (portfolio license, case study rights).

BizDraft generates IP clauses that separate deliverable assignment from background IP retention, with payment-conditioned transfer and clearly scoped portfolio rights.

The pattern

  • Assign specific deliverables, not "all work." "Upon receipt of full payment, Contractor assigns to Client all copyright in the final deliverables listed in Exhibit A."
  • Reserve background IP explicitly. "Contractor retains all rights to pre-existing tools, templates, frameworks, and methods used in the creation of deliverables. Client receives no rights in background IP."
  • Condition transfer on full payment. "IP assignment takes effect only upon receipt of full payment under this agreement, including any amounts due under change orders."
  • Grant a portfolio license. "Contractor may display the deliverables in their portfolio, on their website, and in marketing materials. Contractor will comply with reasonable confidentiality requests regarding unannounced client work."
  • Address work in progress. "Until full payment is received, Client has a non-exclusive license to use completed deliverables for internal review purposes only. Title does not transfer until payment is complete."
