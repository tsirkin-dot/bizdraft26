Deliverables.
The clause that defines what you're actually building.

A deliverable is a concrete artifact with a format, a deadline, and an acceptance criterion. Without all three, "done" is whatever the client decides it is — today.

What it means

In plain English.

A deliverables clause lists the specific outputs the provider will produce: the format of each, the deadline for each, and how the client formally accepts or rejects each one. It transforms "the project" from a vague concept into a list of concrete, checkable items.

Plain English definition

Deliverables are the specific things you hand over at the end: a 3,000-word blog post in Google Docs, a logo in SVG and PNG format, a functional mobile app on TestFlight. The clause names each item, its format, its deadline, and the acceptance process.

When you need it

Any project where you produce a tangible output — documents, designs, code, videos, reports, plans. The deliverables clause is what connects your work to your payment terms: you're owed the next payment when a specific deliverable is accepted.

What happens without it

Without defined deliverables, "the project" is whatever the client imagines it to be. The scope expands silently, acceptance is never formalized, and final payment is attached to a moving target. Clients can always say the project isn't complete.

Why it's missed

Concrete deliverable lists feel tedious to write.

Why missed

The project scope isn't clear yet

At signing, the full list of deliverables may not be determined. The answer isn't to leave it blank — it's to define what's known and reference a statement of work for specifics that will be agreed later.

Why missed

The format seems obvious

Designer assumes Figma source files. Client assumes print-ready PDFs. Developer assumes Git repository. Client assumes hosted app with documentation. What's obvious to each party is different.

Why missed

Acceptance criteria feel bureaucratic

Small-business contracts rarely include formal acceptance procedures. But without them, the client can hold final payment by simply not responding — and "not responding" has no contractual consequence.

What can go wrong

The downstream consequences.

Format disputes

You delivered the logo in JPEG. The client needed a vector file for printing. You believe the format was implied. They believe they're entitled to a redo. Without a format specification, both claims are arguable.

Acceptance never happens

Client receives the deliverable, uses it, and never formally accepts. Six months later they claim it wasn't what they agreed to. Without a defined acceptance process and deemed-acceptance clause, you have no defense.

Payment trigger disputes

Payment is tied to "delivery of final deliverable" but the client claims delivery isn't complete. Without objective acceptance criteria, this argument is decided by who has more leverage — usually the client.

Scope by omission

The deliverable list doesn't mention training, documentation, or handoff. The client expects all three. Without defining what's included in each deliverable, anything adjacent gets absorbed into it.

Red-flag wording

Deliverable language that leaves the door open.

These phrases appear in deliverables clauses constantly. Each one leaves a gap that disputes walk through.

Red flag

"The completed project"

Completed by whose definition? The deliverable must be the thing, not a characterization of its state.

Red flag

"Final files"

Final which files? In what format? At what resolution? Source files or exports only? "Final files" says everything and specifies nothing.

Red flag

"All related assets"

"Related" is a spectrum. The client will define related broadly. Without a specific list, you'll spend hours delivering things you didn't price.

Red flag

"Accepted by client sign-off"

If there's no mechanism to deem delivery accepted absent a response, the client can stall indefinitely by simply not signing off.

Safer pattern

A deliverables clause that defines done.

A strong deliverables clause combines a specific list with format specifications, a delivery timeline, and an acceptance mechanism that includes a deemed-acceptance provision — so silence from the client can't hold your payment hostage.

The pattern

  • List deliverables specifically. "Deliverables under this agreement are: (1) brand logo in SVG, PNG (1x, 2x, 3x), and PDF format; (2) brand style guide as PDF; (3) icon set (10 icons) in SVG."
  • Specify what's excluded from each deliverable. "Deliverables do not include print-ready press files, editable source files in formats other than those listed, or animated variants."
  • Include a delivery timeline. "All deliverables will be delivered on or before [date], or per the milestone schedule in Exhibit A."
  • Define the acceptance window. "Client has 5 business days from delivery to submit written rejection with specific objections. Failure to respond within this window constitutes acceptance."
  • Define what rejection means. "A rejection must specify which deliverable is rejected and the objective basis for rejection. Subjective preference does not constitute a valid rejection basis."
