Limitation of liability.
The clause that decides what a mistake costs.

You charged $5,000 for a project. A bug in your deliverable allegedly cost the client $200,000 in lost sales. Without a limitation of liability clause, both numbers are in play.

What it means

In plain English.

A limitation of liability clause caps the maximum amount either party can recover from the other if something goes wrong. It typically does two things: (1) sets a dollar cap — usually the amount paid under the contract — and (2) excludes certain categories of damages entirely, typically consequential and indirect damages.

Plain English definition

Without a limit, your liability in a contract dispute can exceed the value of the contract by orders of magnitude. The limitation clause says: "Whatever happens, the most I owe you is [X] — typically the total fees paid under this agreement." That's the cap. Everything above it is waived.

When you need it

Every service agreement. The smaller your business relative to your clients, the more critical this clause is. A solo consultant with a $10,000 engagement serving a company with $50M in revenue needs a liability cap more than almost any other clause.

What happens without it

Without a cap, a client can sue for the full consequential damage of any error — lost profits, reputational damage, third-party costs. These figures can vastly exceed the project fee. A single lawsuit can destroy a small business without a limitation clause.

Why it's missed

It sounds like you're planning to screw up. You're not.

Why missed

It signals low confidence

Business owners worry that a liability cap signals they expect to deliver substandard work. But every major commercial contract in every industry includes liability caps — it's standard risk allocation, not an admission of incompetence.

Why missed

The risk feels theoretical

Most projects go fine. A liability clause is for the one that doesn't. And the consequences of being wrong without one are often company-ending — not just expensive.

Why missed

The difference between damages types isn't understood

Direct damages (cost to redo the work) and consequential damages (lost profits, third-party costs) are different categories with very different magnitudes. Without excluding consequential damages, a minor error becomes a major exposure.

What can go wrong

The downstream consequences.

Consequential damage exposure

A website goes down due to a bug in code you wrote. The client claims $300K in lost e-commerce revenue. Your project fee was $15K. Without a consequential damages exclusion, that $300K claim is legally viable.

Unlimited direct damages

Even without consequential damages, "direct damages" can include the cost to hire someone else to redo the work at current market rates — which may significantly exceed the original contract price.

Legal costs alone are devastating

Even winning a lawsuit you could have prevented with a liability cap costs $50,000–200,000 in legal fees. The cap isn't just about the judgment — it's about making the economics of litigation unattractive to the client.

Insurance doesn't cover everything

Professional liability insurance has exclusions, deductibles, and policy limits. A limitation of liability clause is the first line of defense — insurance is the second.

Red-flag wording

Liability language that leaves you exposed.

These formulations appear in service contracts. Each one either removes the cap or leaves a gap large enough to drive a lawsuit through.

Red flag

No liability clause at all

The most common version: simply absent. No cap, no exclusion of consequential damages. Full unlimited liability for everything that might go wrong.

Red flag

"Liability capped at $100"

Courts in many states will void a cap so low it's unconscionable. Set it at the contract value — a defensible, commercially reasonable cap.

Red flag

"Except for gross negligence or willful misconduct"

This carve-out is standard — but clients sometimes try to expand it to "negligence" generally. Gross negligence is a high bar. "Negligence" is a low bar that eats most of the protection the cap provides.

Red flag

"Contractor is liable for all damages arising from the services"

Unlimited liability, explicitly stated. Often buried in one-sided client contracts. Never accept this language without adding a cap.

Safer pattern

A limitation of liability clause that actually limits liability.

A complete limitation of liability clause does two things: sets a dollar cap, and excludes consequential and indirect damages. Courts will enforce both if they're clearly written and not unconscionably low. The cap set at total fees paid is commercially standard and routinely enforced.

The pattern

  • Set the cap at total fees paid. "In no event shall either party's liability to the other exceed the total fees paid by Client to Contractor under this agreement in the twelve months preceding the claim."
  • Exclude consequential damages. "Neither party shall be liable to the other for any indirect, incidental, special, consequential, or punitive damages, including lost profits, loss of data, or business interruption."
  • Include standard exceptions. "These limitations do not apply to: (a) death or bodily injury caused by negligence; (b) fraud or intentional misconduct; or (c) a party's indemnification obligations under this agreement."
  • Make it mutual. The cap should apply to both parties — both as a fairness matter and because courts are more likely to enforce mutual caps than one-sided ones.
  • State it explicitly. "THE PARTIES ACKNOWLEDGE THAT THE LIMITATIONS IN THIS SECTION REFLECT A REASONABLE ALLOCATION OF RISK AND ARE AN ESSENTIAL BASIS FOR THE BARGAIN." All-caps signaling is standard and signals conscious acceptance.
